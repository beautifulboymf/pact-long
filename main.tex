\begin{abstract}
Estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITE) from networked observational data is critical for personalized recommendation, yet existing graph-based methods are monolithic---improvements engineered for one architecture do not transfer to others. We introduce two orthogonal, plug-and-play modules that enhance \emph{any} graph ITE estimator without modifying its internal structure: (1)~\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}, a multi-head cross-attention mechanism that fuses raw node features with node2vec positional embeddings, making community-level structural information explicitly available before GNN aggregation; and (2)~\textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning}, an inverse-variance sample weighting scheme that mitigates heteroscedastic noise in treatment effect regression, with a novel Bernoulli-derived closed-form adaptation for binary outcomes common in recommender systems. We evaluate both modules as drop-in enhancements on eight base models---spanning the full 2016--2025 timeline from BNN to GDC (WSDM~2025 SOTA)---across five graph datasets and six real-world tabular uplift benchmarks, totaling over 1,100 experimental runs. GPE improves PEHE in 69\% of all graph settings, with TARNet+GPE achieving a perfect 11/11 win rate. On continuous outcomes, variance weighting yields a $4\times$ Qini improvement. Under high heteroscedastic noise, the combination of both modules reduces GDC's PEHE by up to 31\%. Our results establish a modular enhancement paradigm: researchers can apply these plug-ins to their preferred base model and expect consistent, complementary improvements for graph-based uplift modeling in recommender systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Accurate estimation of Individual Treatment Effects (ITE) is fundamental for personalized recommendations. Whether a platform decides which users should receive a promotional coupon~\cite{goldenberg2020free,10.1145/3511808.3557100}, which social media posts to boost~\cite{parshakov2020spillover}, or which patients to vaccinate first~\cite{luo2021behavioural}, the core question is the same: \emph{what is the causal effect of this intervention on this specific individual?}

In networked environments such as social platforms and online communities, user behavior is shaped not only by individual characteristics but also by peer influence through network connections~\cite{forastiere2021identification, aral2011creating, DEVALCK2009185}. A user who receives a coupon may influence her friends' purchasing decisions even though they received no coupon themselves---a phenomenon known as \emph{network interference} or \emph{spillover}~\cite{hudgens2008toward, parshakov2020spillover}. This has motivated a growing body of work on graph-based ITE estimation, beginning with NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} and progressing through GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph}, GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum}, IGL~\cite{sui2024invariant}, and the current state-of-the-art GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{images/Uplift_cover.png}
    \caption{Community influence in networked uplift modeling. Treated users (orange) exert spillover effects on their neighbors. Nodes in the same community (shaded regions) share structural roles that standard GNN message-passing cannot distinguish from local neighborhood similarity. GPE captures these community-level positional signals.}
    \label{fig:cover}
\end{figure}

Despite steady progress, we identify three fundamental challenges that limit the practical impact of this line of research:

\textbf{Challenge 1: No positional awareness.} Standard GNN backbones aggregate neighbor features through message-passing, but they lack explicit awareness of a node's global structural position~\cite{velickovic2017graph}. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:cover}, nodes in the same community may share confounding patterns---e.g., users in a fitness community may have correlated health behaviors---that local aggregation alone cannot capture. This structural information is precisely what is needed to reduce confounding bias in ITE estimation.

\textbf{Challenge 2: Heteroscedasticity.} Treatment effect estimation inherently involves heteroscedastic noise: the variance of the outcome may differ across individuals due to unobserved moderators. Standard MSE losses treat all samples equally, allowing high-variance observations to dominate gradient updates and degrade estimation quality. While variance-aware methods have been studied in tabular settings~\cite{SHI2024111679, mo2022efficient, abrevaya2023estimation}, they have not been integrated with graph-based ITE estimators.

\textbf{Challenge 3: Monolithic architectures.} Each new graph ITE method proposes a complete end-to-end architecture. An improvement designed for one model---say, GDC's treatment-conditioned aggregation---cannot be transferred to another model like GIAL. This forces practitioners to choose a single architecture rather than combining the best ideas, and it makes incremental progress difficult to accumulate across the community.

\textbf{Our approach.} We address all three challenges by shifting from ``build a better model'' to ``build better modules.'' We propose two orthogonal, plug-and-play components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}: A cross-attention mechanism that fuses node features with node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} positional embeddings at the \emph{input level}, before any GNN processing.
    \item \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning (Var)}: An inverse-variance sample weighting scheme applied at the \emph{loss level}, after the prediction heads.
\end{itemize}

Neither module modifies the base model's internal architecture. GPE replaces the model's input; Var re-weights its loss. This design ensures that both modules compose with \emph{any} graph ITE estimator.

We validate this universality claim through what we believe is the most comprehensive ``control variable'' evaluation in the graph-based uplift literature: each module is independently applied to eight base models across eleven datasets, producing over 1,100 experimental runs. Our key findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{GPE is a universal graph-uplift enhancer.} It improves PEHE in 69\% of all (model, dataset, noise) cells, including 7 of 8 tested base models. TARNet+GPE achieves a perfect 11/11 win rate.
    \item \textbf{Variance weighting complements GPE under heteroscedasticity.} At high noise ($\rho\!=\!30$), GPE alone can overfit, but adding Var stabilizes learning. GDC+GPE+Var achieves PEHE of 2.99 on PubMed (vs.\ 4.31 vanilla, a 31\% reduction).
    \item \textbf{On real-world tabular data}, Var yields a $4\times$ Qini improvement on continuous outcomes (Hillstrom spend: 0.029$\to$0.117).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{ITE estimation and uplift modeling.}
Meta-learners form the backbone of modern ITE estimation. The S-learner fits a single model $\mu(x,t)$; the T-learner fits separate models per treatment arm~\cite{shalit2017estimating}; the X-learner~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners} uses cross-residuals for improved efficiency when treatment groups are imbalanced; and the DR-learner~\cite{kennedy2023towards} combines outcome regression with propensity weighting for doubly robust estimation. Deep learning extensions include BNN and TARNet/CFRNet~\cite{johansson2016learning, shalit2017estimating}, which learn balanced representations to reduce selection bias, SNet~\cite{curth2021nonparametric} for nonparametric estimation, and EFIN~\cite{liu2023explicit} for explicit feature interactions in marketing applications. Uplift trees~\cite{radcliffe2011real} and BART~\cite{mcculloch2018bart} provide non-neural alternatives. All these methods assume independent samples and do not account for network interference.

\textbf{Causal inference under network interference.}
The foundational framework of partial interference~\cite{hudgens2008toward} assumes that interference occurs only within pre-defined clusters. Graph-cluster randomization~\cite{ugander2013graph} extends this to network settings. In the deep learning era, NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} pioneered GCN-based causal inference from networked observational data by learning balanced representations with Wasserstein distance regularization. GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} improved upon this by incorporating mutual information maximization and adversarial balancing between treatment and control representations. GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} proposed a transformed-target estimator for direct uplift prediction. IGL~\cite{sui2024invariant} introduced environment-invariant representations to improve generalization across different graph substructures. Most recently, GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} achieves SOTA performance by disentangling node features into adjustment and confounder factors with treatment-conditioned graph aggregation. Related approaches include modeling interference explicitly~\cite{huang2023modeling}, doubly robust estimation on networks~\cite{chen2024doubly}, and hypergraph causal models~\cite{ma2022learning}. Generalization bounds have been studied by~\citet{cai2023generalization}.

\textbf{Heteroscedasticity in treatment effect estimation.}
The statistical literature has long recognized that outcome variance may vary across individuals and that ignoring this heteroscedasticity reduces estimation efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936}. Recent work has studied this in the causal inference context: \citet{SHI2024111679} propose model averaging under heteroscedasticity, \citet{mo2022efficient} develop efficient individualized treatment rules for heteroscedastic models, and \citet{abrevaya2023estimation} study endogenous heteroscedasticity. Multi-task uncertainty weighting~\cite{kendall2018multi} addresses a related problem in computer vision. However, none of these approaches have been integrated with graph-based ITE estimators.

\textbf{Positioning.} Unlike all prior work in graph-based ITE estimation, which proposes a new monolithic architecture, we contribute two \emph{modular enhancements} and validate them across the full spectrum of existing models---from NetDeconf~(2020) to GDC~(2025)---establishing their architecture-agnostic utility.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Uplift Modeling and ITE}

Consider a population of $n$ users indexed by $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$. Each user has pre-treatment covariates $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, receives a binary treatment $T_i \in \{0,1\}$, and exhibits potential outcomes $Y_i(0)$ (control) and $Y_i(1)$ (treated). The observed outcome follows the switching equation $Y_i = T_i Y_i(1) + (1 - T_i) Y_i(0)$. The \emph{Individual Treatment Effect} (ITE) is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ite}
    D_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0),
\end{equation}
and the \emph{Conditional Average Treatment Effect} (CATE) is $\tau(x) = \mathbb{E}[D_i \mid X_i = x]$. The goal of uplift modeling is to estimate $\hat{\tau}$ that minimizes the Precision in Estimation of Heterogeneous Effects:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pehe}
    \epsilon_{\text{PEHE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\tau(X_i) - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Graph-Based ITE under Network Interference}

When users are connected via a social graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ with adjacency matrix $\mathcal{A}$, the potential outcome framework must account for \emph{network interference}: user $i$'s outcome may depend on neighbors' treatments. Under the \emph{neighborhood interference} assumption~\cite{forastiere2021identification}, we have $Y_i = Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i)$, where $\mathcal{T}_i$ summarizes multi-hop neighbor treatment information. The estimation objective becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:graph_ite}
    \hat{\tau} = \arg\min_{\hat{\tau}} \; \mathbb{E}\!\left[(\tau - \hat{\tau})^2 \mid X = X_i, \, X_{\mathcal{N}} = X_{\mathcal{N}}^i\right],
\end{equation}
where $X_{\mathcal{N}}^i$ denotes the features of $i$'s graph neighbors, and the expectation is conditional on both individual and neighborhood covariates.

Standard assumptions in this setting are: (i)~\emph{network unconfoundedness}---treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes given observed individual and neighborhood covariates; (ii)~\emph{overlap}---every user has a non-trivial probability of receiving either treatment; (iii)~\emph{neighborhood interference}---outcomes depend on the graph only through a summary of $k$-hop neighbor treatments~\cite{guo2020learning, hudgens2008toward}.

\subsection{X-Learner}

The X-learner~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners} is a meta-learning strategy particularly suited for settings with imbalanced treatment and control groups. It proceeds in three stages: (1)~fit conditional outcome models $\hat{\mu}_t(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid T\!=\!t, X\!=\!x, X_{\mathcal{N}}]$ for each treatment arm $t \in \{0,1\}$; (2)~compute cross-residuals $\mathrm{D}_1^i = Y_i(1) - \hat{\mu}_0(X_i)$ and $\mathrm{D}_0^i = \hat{\mu}_1(X_i) - Y_i(0)$, then fit regressors $g_0, g_1$ on these pseudo-outcomes; (3)~combine using the propensity score: $\hat{\tau}(x) = e(x) \cdot g_0(x) + (1-e(x)) \cdot g_1(x)$. We use the X-learner as one of our base models and also as the meta-learning backbone in our combined CAVIN configuration.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

We present two plug-in modules designed for composability: GPE operates at the input level and Var operates at the loss level (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}). Crucially, neither modifies the internal architecture of any base model---they enhance it from the outside.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}\label{sec:gpe}

Standard GNN message-passing aggregates local neighborhoods but is inherently limited in capturing global structural roles. Two nodes in distinct communities may have isomorphic local neighborhoods yet occupy fundamentally different positions in the graph, leading to different confounding patterns. GPE addresses this limitation by explicitly injecting positional information before GNN processing.

\textbf{Positional embeddings.} We compute $d_p$-dimensional positional embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$ via node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec}, which captures both local (BFS-like) and global (DFS-like) structural roles through biased random walks. These embeddings encode community membership, bridge roles, and hierarchical position---exactly the structural information relevant for confounding in networked causal inference.

\textbf{Cross-attention fusion.} Given raw node features $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ and positional embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$, GPE computes queries from features and keys/values from positions:
\begin{align}
    Q &= hW_Q, \quad K = pW_K, \quad V = pW_V, \label{eq:qkv}\\
    \text{head}_j &= \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q_j K_j^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V_j, \label{eq:head}\\
    \text{MHA}(Q,K,V) &= [\text{head}_1; \ldots; \text{head}_H] W_O, \label{eq:mha}
\end{align}
where $W_Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d_k H}$, $W_K, W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_p \times d_k H}$, and $W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k H \times d_m}$ are learnable projections, $H$ is the number of attention heads, and $d_k = d_m / H$. The output is combined with the original features via a residual connection and layer normalization:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gpe}
    \text{GPE}(h, p) = \text{LayerNorm}\!\left([h;\, \text{MHA}(Q,K,V)] + f_{\text{res}}([h;\, p])\right),
\end{equation}
where $[\,\cdot\,;\,\cdot\,]$ denotes concatenation along the feature dimension and $f_{\text{res}}: \mathbb{R}^{d_h + d_p} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_h + d_m}$ is a learned linear residual projection that ensures dimensional compatibility.

\textbf{Plug-in interface.} For any model $M$ that takes node features $X$ as input, the GPE-enhanced version is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gpe_plugin}
    M_{\text{GPE}}(X, \mathcal{A}) = M(\text{GPE}(X, p),\; \mathcal{A}).
\end{equation}
The base model $M$ requires \emph{no modification}---GPE simply transforms its input. This is why GPE can be applied to architectures as different as TARNet (which has separate treatment heads) and GDC (which has disentangled factor representations).

\subsection{Variance-Weighted Learning}\label{sec:var}

\textbf{Heteroscedastic decomposition.} Under heteroscedasticity, the observed ITE decomposes as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hetero}
    D_i = \tau(X_i) + \varepsilon_i, \quad \text{where } \text{Var}[\varepsilon_i \mid X_i] = \sigma^2(X_i)
\end{equation}
varies across individuals. The noise $\varepsilon_i$ arises from unobserved moderators, measurement error, and the fundamental variability of individual responses to treatment. Standard MSE loss treats all samples equally, effectively allowing high-variance samples---whose observed effects are dominated by noise---to exert disproportionate influence on gradient updates.

\textbf{Inverse-variance weighting.} We propose re-weighting samples by the inverse of their estimated outcome variance:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weights}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\max\{\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i),\; \delta\}},
\end{equation}
where $\delta > 0$ is a small constant preventing division by zero (we use $\delta = 0.01$ throughout). The weighted regression objective becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wls}
    \hat{\tau}_{\text{WLS}} = \arg\min_{\hat{\tau}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \left(\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i)\right)^2.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Variance estimation for continuous outcomes.} When the outcome $Y$ is continuous, we estimate $\sigma^2$ via a dedicated MLP head that predicts $\log \hat{\sigma}^2(X_i)$ (exponentiated for positivity). The training target for the variance head is derived from the residual decomposition:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:var_target}
    \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{D}_i^2 \mid X_i] = \tau^2(X_i) + \sigma^2(X_i),
\end{equation}
so we use $\mathrm{D}_i^2 - \hat{\tau}^2(X_i)$ as the regression target for $\hat{\sigma}^2$, with $\hat{\tau}$ detached from the computation graph to prevent gradient contamination between the treatment effect and variance estimation objectives.

\textbf{Bernoulli adaptation for binary outcomes.} In recommendation settings (click, conversion, purchase), outcomes are binary. For binary $Y \in \{0,1\}$, the noise variance is analytically determined by the Bernoulli distribution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bernoulli}
    \sigma_t^2(X_i) = \hat{\mu}_t(X_i)\bigl(1 - \hat{\mu}_t(X_i)\bigr), \quad t \in \{0,1\},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mu}_t(X_i)$ is the model's predicted conditional outcome probability. This requires \emph{no additional parameters}---the variance is derived from the model's existing predictions. This adaptation is, to our knowledge, the first closed-form variance weighting scheme for binary-outcome uplift modeling.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:blue}
Under heteroscedasticity with known variance function $\sigma^2(X_i)$, the weighted least squares estimator~\eqref{eq:wls} achieves $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{WLS}}) \preceq \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ in the L\"{o}wner order, i.e., it is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE) by the generalized Gauss--Markov theorem~\cite{Aitken_1936}. Full proof is provided in the anonymous code repository.
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Plug-in interface.} For any model $M$ with outcome loss $\mathcal{L}$, the variance-weighted version replaces per-sample losses with their weighted counterparts:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:var_plugin}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot \mathcal{L}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i},
\end{equation}
where weights $w_i$ are computed from the model's own predictions (detached). Normalization by $\sum w_i$ preserves the loss scale across different variance regimes.

\subsection{Combined Plug-in Architecture}\label{sec:combined}

Because GPE and Var operate at different levels (input vs.\ loss), they compose naturally:
\begin{equation}
    M_{\text{GPE+Var}}(X, \mathcal{A}) = M\bigl(\text{GPE}(X, p),\; \mathcal{A}\bigr) \;\text{with loss}\; \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}.
\end{equation}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:plugin} formalizes the complete training procedure. When both modules are applied to an X-learner backbone, we refer to the resulting system as CAVIN (\textbf{CA}usal \textbf{V}ariance-weighted \textbf{I}nference on \textbf{N}etworks).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
    \caption{Plug-in Training Procedure}
    \label{alg:plugin}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} Graph $\mathcal{G}=(\mathcal{V},\mathcal{E})$, data $\{X_i, T_i, Y_i\}_{i=1}^n$, base model $M$
        \STATE \textbf{Flags:} \textsc{UseGPE}, \textsc{UseVar}
        \STATE Compute node2vec embeddings $p \leftarrow \text{Node2Vec}(\mathcal{G})$
        \FOR{epoch $= 1, \ldots, E$}
            \IF{\textsc{UseGPE}}
                \STATE $\tilde{X} \leftarrow \text{GPE}(X, p)$ \hfill \COMMENT{Eq.~\ref{eq:gpe}}
            \ELSE
                \STATE $\tilde{X} \leftarrow X$
            \ENDIF
            \STATE $\hat{\mu}_0, \hat{\mu}_1, \hat{e} \leftarrow M(\tilde{X}, \mathcal{A})$ \hfill \COMMENT{Base model forward}
            \STATE Compute base loss $\mathcal{L}_i$ for each sample $i$
            \IF{\textsc{UseVar}}
                \STATE Estimate $\hat{\sigma}^2_i$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:var_target} or~\ref{eq:bernoulli})
                \STATE $w_i \leftarrow 1 / \max(\hat{\sigma}^2_i, \delta)$ \hfill \COMMENT{Eq.~\ref{eq:weights}}
                \STATE $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \sum_i w_i \mathcal{L}_i / \sum_i w_i$ \hfill \COMMENT{Eq.~\ref{eq:var_plugin}}
            \ELSE
                \STATE $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \mathcal{L}_i$
            \ENDIF
            \STATE Update all parameters via $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ (Adam)
        \ENDFOR
        \STATE \textbf{Output:} Enhanced model $M$ with trained plug-in modules
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}
%% ====================================================================

We design experiments to answer three research questions: \textbf{RQ1}~Does GPE universally improve graph ITE estimators? \textbf{RQ2}~Does variance weighting complement GPE under heteroscedastic noise? \textbf{RQ3}~Does variance weighting transfer to real-world tabular uplift tasks?

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Graph datasets.} We use five graph datasets with semi-synthetic outcome generation. Three large citation/co-authorship networks---CoraFull (19,793 nodes)~\cite{bojchevski2018deep}, DBLP (17,716 nodes), and PubMed (19,717 nodes)---are evaluated under heteroscedastic noise levels $\rho \in \{5, 10, 30\}$, yielding 9 evaluation cells per model. We also include the standard BlogCatalog (5,196 nodes) and Flickr (7,575 nodes) benchmarks from~\citet{guo2020learning} to ensure comparability with prior work (2 additional cells). Our semi-synthetic data generating process extends prior work by incorporating Louvain-detected communities~\cite{blondel2008fast}, multi-hop treatment spillover (3-hop), and per-node heteroscedastic noise scaled by $\rho$.

\textbf{Tabular datasets.} We evaluate on six real-world uplift benchmarks without graph structure: Criteo Uplift (1M rows, binary conversion), Hillstrom E-mail (42K rows, binary visit and continuous spend), RetailHero (200K, binary purchase), Lenta (690K, binary response), and X5 (200K, binary purchase with enriched features). These datasets cover the dominant use cases in recommender-system uplift modeling: e-commerce conversion, email marketing response, and promotional campaign effectiveness.

\textbf{Base models.} We test eight models spanning the full evolution of graph ITE research:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Classical meta-learners}: BNN (S-learner)~\cite{johansson2016learning}, TARNet (T-learner)~\cite{shalit2017estimating}, X-learner~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners}
    \item \emph{Graph ITE models}: NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM 2020), GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} (KDD 2021), GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} (2023), GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM 2025)
    \item \emph{Combined}: CAVIN (X-learner + GPE + Var)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Training and evaluation.} All models use Adam optimizer with learning rate $10^{-3}$, trained for 200 epochs with 60/20/20 train/validation/test splits. Graph experiments use 3 random seeds; tabular experiments use 5 seeds. We report $\sqrt{\epsilon_{\text{PEHE}}}$ (lower is better) for semi-synthetic graph data and Qini coefficient (higher is better) for real-world tabular data. GPE uses $H\!=\!4$ attention heads, $d_p\!=\!128$ for node2vec, and $d_k\!=\!32$.

In this architecture, the positional information provided by GPE cannot differentially condition the treatment vs.\ control representations---both arms share the same positional context, nullifying the structural signal.

\textbf{Finding 3: GPE improves the current SOTA.} GDC+GPE reduces PEHE by 15\% on DBLP ($\rho\!=\!10$: $3.19 \to 2.70$) and by 8\% on PubMed ($3.06 \to 2.82$). \textbf{GPE alone can hurt under high noise.} At $\rho\!=\!30$, GPE \emph{degrades} GDC on CoraFull ($4.93 \to 5.90$, +20\%) and PubMed ($4.31 \to 4.80$, +11\%). \textbf{Var rescues GPE and enables synergy.} Adding variance weighting to GDC+GPE not only reverses the degradation but produces the best results across all three datasets. On PubMed, GDC+GPE+Var achieves $\sqrt{\text{PEHE}} = 2.99$, a 31\% improvement over vanilla GDC ($4.31$) and a 38\% improvement over GPE-only ($4.80$). \textbf{Var alone provides modest but consistent gains.} Without GPE, variance weighting reduces GDC's PEHE on CoraFull (12\%) and PubMed (12\%), while slightly increasing it on DBLP (+1\%). This confirms Var's role as a stabilizer under heteroscedasticity rather than a source of new information.

This is the ideal case: GPE provides richer input representations, and Var prevents the model from overfitting to noisy labels. TARNet+GPE+Var achieves 4.27, a 33\% reduction from the vanilla 6.33, with neither module alone achieving more than a 17\% reduction.

\textbf{Pattern B: Var dominance (X-learner).} For the X-learner, Var alone achieves the best result (3.84), and adding GPE slightly hurts (4.14). \section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

We have introduced Graph Positional Encoding and Variance-Weighted Learning as two orthogonal, architecture-agnostic plug-in modules for graph-based uplift modeling. Through evaluation on 8 base models, 11 datasets, and over 1,100 runs, we establish three main results: (1)~GPE is a universal graph-uplift enhancer, improving 7 of 8 models with a 69\% overall win rate; (2)~variance weighting provides complementary gains under heteroscedastic noise, with the GPE+Var combination reducing PEHE by up to 31\%; and (3)~on real-world tabular data, variance weighting achieves a $4\times$ Qini improvement on continuous outcomes.

Rather than proposing another monolithic model, our work establishes a \emph{modular enhancement paradigm}: researchers and practitioners can apply GPE and variance weighting to their preferred base model---whether a classical meta-learner or the latest graph ITE architecture---and expect consistent, interpretable improvements. We believe this composability is the most scalable path toward better uplift modeling in recommender systems.

\textbf{Limitations.} GPE's effectiveness depends on node2vec embedding quality, which may degrade on very sparse or disconnected graphs. Variance weighting is less effective for binary outcomes with extreme class imbalance. We leave the exploration of alternative positional encodings (e.g., spectral, random-walk structural) and adaptive variance estimation schedules to future work.
