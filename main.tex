\begin{abstract}
Estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITE) from networked observational data is crucial for personalized recommendation, yet existing graph-based methods are monolithic---improvements to one architecture do not transfer to others. We introduce two orthogonal, plug-and-play modules that enhance \emph{any} graph ITE estimator: (1)~Graph Positional Encoding (GPE), a multi-head self-attention fusion of node features and node2vec positional embeddings that captures community-level structural information, and (2)~Variance-Weighted Learning, an inverse-variance sample weighting scheme that mitigates heteroscedastic noise, with both a learned variant for continuous outcomes and an analytical Bernoulli variant for binary outcomes. We conduct a controlled ``plug-in'' evaluation on eight base models---from the foundational NetDeconf (WSDM 2020) to the current state-of-the-art GDC (WSDM 2025)---across five graph datasets and six real-world tabular uplift benchmarks, totaling over 1,100 experimental runs. GPE improves PEHE in 69\% of all graph settings, with TARNet+GPE achieving a perfect 11/11 win rate. On continuous real-world outcomes, variance weighting yields a 4$\times$ Qini improvement. At high noise, the combination of both modules reduces GDC's PEHE by up to 31\%. Our results establish these modules as a general-purpose enhancement paradigm for graph-based uplift modeling.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/Uplift_cover.png}
    \caption{Mutual influence among users in a social network. Users in community~1 (blue) and community~2 (green) exhibit different treatment response patterns. The orange user is the estimation target. Grey shading indicates higher outcome noise (heteroscedasticity).}
    \label{fig:cover}
\end{figure}

Modern recommender systems routinely face a decision that individual-level modeling alone cannot answer: \emph{which users will genuinely respond to an intervention, as opposed to those who would have acted the same regardless?} Answering this requires accurate estimation of the Individual Treatment Effect (ITE) and Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE). The stakes are substantial---digital platforms collectively spend hundreds of billions of dollars annually on targeted incentives~\cite{goldenberg2020free, parshakov2020spillover}, yet misallocated promotions not only waste budget but actively harm user experience. On social platforms, this problem is compounded by network interference: a user's response to a recommendation is shaped not only by their own preferences but also by the simultaneous actions of their social connections~\cite{aral2011creating}. Empirical studies find that users in tightly-knit communities respond up to 2.3$\times$ more strongly to identical promotions than socially isolated users~\cite{luo2021behavioural, DEVALCK2009185}, a phenomenon that ITE estimators assuming i.i.d.\ data~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards, shalit2017estimating} systematically fail to capture. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:cover}, community membership and outcome noise are both spatially structured in real social networks---making them two aspects of the same underlying challenge that demand a unified treatment.

While Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have enabled the integration of network structures into causal inference, three major challenges remain. \emph{First}, existing GNN-based ITE estimators treat all neighbors uniformly during message passing, overlooking the hierarchical nature of community-driven interactions. The lack of explicit positional encoding fails to disentangle the influence of high-level community biases from local node interactions~\cite{10621010}. \emph{Second}, real-world outcomes frequently exhibit heteroscedasticity~\cite{abrevaya2023estimation}: the behavior patterns of some users may become highly unstable due to measurement errors or anomalous factors, causing significant randomness in treatment outcomes that degrades model performance if left unaddressed. While variance-weighted methods~\cite{SHI2024111679, mo2022efficient} have been explored in traditional ITE estimation, their integration with graph-based approaches remains nascent. \emph{Third}, and perhaps most fundamentally, each new graph ITE model proposes a \emph{monolithic} architecture whose innovations are tightly coupled to the specific design. An improvement that works for one model cannot be trivially transferred to another, limiting cumulative progress in the field.

To address these gaps, we take a different approach from the prevailing paradigm of designing yet another end-to-end architecture. Instead, we propose two orthogonal, \emph{plug-and-play} modules that enhance any existing graph ITE estimator: (1)~\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}, a self-attention mechanism that fuses node features with node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} positional embeddings to capture community effects before GNN processing; and (2)~\textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning}, which estimates per-sample ITE variance and reweights the regression loss accordingly, with theoretical guarantees from the Gauss--Markov theorem~\cite{Aitken_1936}. Crucially, GPE operates at the \emph{input} level and variance weighting at the \emph{loss} level---neither modifies the base model's internal architecture.

We validate this modular paradigm through a rigorous controlled evaluation across eight base models---spanning the foundational NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM 2020) to the current SOTA GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM 2025)---on five graph and six tabular datasets, totaling over 1,100 experimental runs. Our key findings are: (i)~GPE improves 7 of 8 base models, with TARNet+GPE achieving a \emph{perfect} 11/11 win rate across all graph settings; (ii)~Variance weighting provides complementary gains under heteroscedastic noise, yielding a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous real-world outcomes; (iii)~The combination reduces GDC's PEHE by up to 31\% at high noise. Code and full results are available at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CAVIN}.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{ITE estimation and uplift modeling.}
Traditional approaches encompass tree-based methods~\cite{radcliffe2011real, athey2016recursive}, knapsack formulations~\cite{goldenberg2020free, 10.1145/3511808.3557100}, and meta-learners~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards}. Deep learning extensions---BNN~\cite{johansson2016learning}, TARNet/CFRNet~\cite{shalit2017estimating}, SNet~\cite{curth2021nonparametric}, TNet~\cite{chen2024doubly}, and EFIN~\cite{liu2023explicit}---primarily adopt the meta-learner framework, utilizing neural networks to estimate the components defined by S-, T-, X-, or DR-learners. However, in domains such as online marketing, samples often violate the i.i.d.\ assumption, making network interference a critical frontier.

\textbf{Causal inference under network interference.}
Early work by Hudgens \& Halloran~\cite{hudgens2008toward} introduced partial interference within predefined clusters. Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning} pioneered GCN-based network deconfounding with Wasserstein representation balancing, establishing the BlogCatalog and Flickr benchmarks. Ma et al.~\cite{ma2021causal} proposed representation learning combined with GNNs for causal inference under networked interference and intervention policy enhancement. Huang et al.~\cite{huang2023modeling} modeled interference for ITE estimation using attention-based GCN aggregation. GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} incorporated mutual-information maximization and adversarial balancing to handle imbalanced network structures. GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} proposed a transformed-target estimator for direct uplift prediction. More recently, Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2024learning} combined sample weighting with attention-based methods to model heterogeneous interference in causal inference on networks. IGL~\cite{sui2024invariant} introduced environment-invariant representations for inductive cross-domain settings. The current state-of-the-art, GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc}, disentangles node features into adjustment and confounder representations via learnable masks and treatment-conditioned graph aggregators. A common thread across all these methods is that each proposes a complete, indivisible architecture---our work takes the orthogonal approach of developing modular components that can enhance any of them.

\textbf{Positional and structural encoding in GNNs.}
Standard message-passing GNNs are inherently limited in distinguishing structurally equivalent nodes that occupy different global positions~\cite{velickovic2017graph}. To address this, random walk positional encodings (RWPE) and learned structural positional encodings (LSPE) have been integrated into graph transformers. Node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} generates position-aware embeddings via biased random walks and has been widely adopted for link prediction and node classification. However, to our knowledge, positional encodings have not been studied as a plug-in enhancement for \emph{causal} GNNs, where the treatment-heterogeneous setting creates unique demands: positional context must differentially condition the representations for treatment and control groups, rather than merely augmenting a single prediction task.

\textbf{Heteroscedastic noise in deep learning.}
Modeling input-dependent noise variance has a rich history in neural networks, from the seminal work of Nix \& Weigend (1994) on heteroscedastic regression to the multi-task uncertainty weighting of Kendall \& Gal~\cite{kendall2018multi}. In the ITE literature, variance-weighted methods have been explored for tabular data~\cite{SHI2024111679, mo2022efficient, abrevaya2023estimation}, but their integration with graph-based approaches remains unexplored. Our variance head predicts $\log \sigma^2$ directly (avoiding numerical instability from negative variance estimates) and adapts to binary outcomes via the analytical Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$, bridging the gap between heteroscedastic regression and uplift modeling on networks.

\textbf{Our positioning.} Unlike prior work that introduces a new monolithic model, we contribute two modular enhancements---one at the input level (GPE) and one at the loss level (variance weighting)---and validate them across the full spectrum of existing architectures, from the 2020 foundational work to the 2025 SOTA. To our knowledge, this is the first controlled ``plug-in'' evaluation in graph-based ITE estimation.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Graph-Based ITE Estimation}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ represent a graph with node set $\mathcal{V}$ and edge set $\mathcal{E}$. Each node $i$ is characterized by a feature vector $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, a treatment indicator $T_i \in \{0,1\}$, and an observed outcome $Y_i$. We define the dataset as $\mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, T_i, Y_i), \mathcal{A}\}_{i=1}^{n}$, where $\mathcal{A}$ denotes the adjacency matrix. Unlike the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA) used in traditional ITE, outcomes in networks are affected by peer effects. We adopt the following assumptions:

\begin{assumption}[Network Consistency]
The potential outcome $Y_i$ depends on the individual's own treatment $T_i$ and an aggregation of their neighbors' treatments: $Y_i = Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i)$, where $\mathcal{T}_i = \mathrm{agg}(T_{\mathcal{N}_i^{1,\ldots,r}})$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Unconfoundedness]
Conditional on individual features $X_i$ and multi-hop neighbor features $X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$, treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes: $Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i) \perp (T_i, T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Overlap]
Every treatment combination has non-zero probability: $0 < p(T_i, T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i} \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) < 1$.
\end{assumption}

The objective is to estimate the Network CATE: $\tau(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}]$. Since the ground truth $\tau$ is unobservable, we rely on a proxy variable $\tilde{D}$ (imputed treatment effect) for optimization:

\begin{theorem}[Optimization Equivalence]\label{thm:equiv}
If\, $\mathbb{E}[\tilde{D} \mid X] = \tau(X)$, then $\arg\min_{\hat{\tau}} \mathbb{E}[(\tilde{D} - \hat{\tau})^2] = \arg\min_{\hat{\tau}} \mathbb{E}[(\tau - \hat{\tau})^2]$.
\end{theorem}

This justifies using imputed residuals as regression targets in the X-learner framework, which estimates $\mu_t = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid T=t, X, X_{\mathcal{N}}]$ for $t \in \{0,1\}$, computes cross-residuals $\mathrm{D}_1 = Y(1) - \mu_0$ and $\mathrm{D}_0 = \mu_1 - Y(0)$, and combines them via propensity-weighted regression.

\subsection{Graph Attention Networks}

To model complex network interference, we adopt multi-head Graph Attention Networks (GATs)~\cite{velickovic2017graph}. Each node's representation is iteratively updated by aggregating transformed features from its neighborhood:
\begin{equation}
    h_v^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left(\sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \alpha_{vu}^{(l)} W^{(l)} h_u^{(l)}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{vu}^{(l)} = \mathrm{softmax}_u\!\left(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^T [W^{(l)}h_v \| W^{(l)}h_u])\right)$ are learned attention coefficients. By stacking $L=5$ GAT layers, each node's representation captures information from its 5-hop neighborhood, contextualizing local treatment effects within the broader community structure.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

We present two plug-in modules. GPE operates at the \emph{input} level; variance weighting operates at the \emph{loss} level. Neither modifies the internal architecture of any base model.

\subsection{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}\label{sec:gpe}

Standard GNNs aggregate local neighborhoods but lack explicit awareness of a node's global position. Two nodes with isomorphic local structures may belong to different communities with distinct treatment response patterns. GPE addresses this by fusing raw features $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ with pre-computed node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$ via multi-head cross-attention, where features serve as queries and positional embeddings as keys and values:
\begin{align}
    Q &= hW_Q,\; K = pW_K,\; V = pW_V, \label{eq:qkv} \\
    \mathrm{head}_k &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(Q_k K_k^\top / \sqrt{d_k}\right) V_k, \label{eq:head}\\
    z &= \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl([h;\; W_o\,\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_H)] + f_{\mathrm{res}}([h;p])\bigr), \label{eq:fuse}
\end{align}
where $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_o$ are learnable projections, LN denotes layer normalization, $[\cdot;\cdot]$ is concatenation, and $f_{\mathrm{res}}$ is a residual projection. The output $z$ replaces $h$ as the input to any downstream GNN backbone.

\textbf{Plug-in interface.} For any base model $M$ that takes node features $X$ as input, the GPE-enhanced version is simply $M_{\text{+GPE}}(\cdot) = M(\mathrm{GPE}(X, p), \cdot)$. The node2vec embeddings are computed once offline and remain fixed during training, adding negligible runtime overhead.

\subsection{Variance-Weighted Learning}\label{sec:var}

Standard meta-learners treat all samples equally, implicitly assuming homoscedastic noise. In practice, user behavior volatility varies significantly---some users are consistent while others are erratic. We model the observed pseudo-treatment effect as $D_i = \tau(X_i) + \varepsilon_i$, where $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_i \mid X_i] = \sigma^2(X_i)$ is feature-dependent.

To estimate $\sigma^2(X_i)$, we use the identity $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[D^2 \mid X] - \tau^2(X)$ and train a dedicated variance head. A critical implementation detail is that this head predicts $\log \sigma^2$ directly (rather than $\sigma^2$ itself), with the actual variance recovered via exponentiation. This log-parameterization serves two purposes: it guarantees that all variance estimates are strictly positive regardless of the head's output, and it naturally matches the scale of the regression target, preventing the degenerate solutions that arise when unconstrained variance heads collapse to zero. The regression target is $\log((\mathrm{D} - \hat{\tau})^2 + \epsilon)$ with a small $\epsilon$ for numerical stability, using a pilot estimate $\hat{\tau}$ from the outcome heads with gradients detached to prevent contamination. The sample weights are:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\max\{\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i), \delta\}}, \quad \delta > 0,
\end{equation}
and the refined ITE estimator solves $\hat{\tau} = \arg\min_{\hat{\tau}'} \sum_{i} w_i (\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}'(X_i))^2$.

\textbf{Binary outcome adaptation.} For binary outcomes (e.g., click/conversion in recommendation), the noise variance is analytically determined by the Bernoulli model: $\sigma^2_t(X_i) = \mu_t(X_i)(1 - \mu_t(X_i))$, requiring no learned variance head.

\begin{remark}[Theoretical Motivation via Local Linearization]
We emphasize that the following analysis provides \emph{theoretical motivation} rather than a formal guarantee for the nonlinear deep learning setting. The final layer of any neural network is a linear regressor on learned features $\phi(X)$. If we treat $\phi$ as fixed (i.e., analyze the last-layer regression conditional on the learned representation), the problem reduces to linear regression where the Gauss--Markov theorem applies directly. More generally, by Taylor's theorem, $\tau(X)$ can be locally approximated as linear within a neighborhood of each data point. Under either interpretation, the Gauss--Markov theorem guarantees that the WLS estimator achieves $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{WLS}}) \preceq \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ in the Löwner order~\cite{Aitken_1936}. A further gap between theory and practice is that we use \emph{estimated} (not true) variance $\hat{\sigma}^2$ for weighting; analyzing the bias-variance trade-off of plug-in WLS with estimated weights in the deep learning regime is an open theoretical question that we do not resolve here. Instead, we rely on the extensive empirical evidence in Section~\ref{sec:exp}---particularly the consistent improvements at high noise ($\rho{=}30$)---as the primary justification for the variance weighting mechanism.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{images/Uplift.png}
    \caption{The overall architecture. \textbf{Left:} Comparison with general learners (S/T-Learner) that assume i.i.d.\ data. \textbf{Center:} The standard X-Learner uses static propensity weights. \textbf{Right:} Our framework adds two plug-in modules---GPE for community-aware feature fusion (input level) and variance-weighted loss for adaptive noise mitigation (loss level)---on top of any base model.}
    \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure*}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}, our framework enhances any base model $M$ through two orthogonal plug-ins. First, node2vec embeddings are fused with raw features via GPE (Eqs.~\ref{eq:qkv}--\ref{eq:fuse}). The fused features are processed by the base model's GNN backbone and output heads. Finally, the outcome loss is reweighted by inverse variance estimates. The complete training procedure is formalized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavin}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
    \caption{Plug-in Training Procedure}
    \label{alg:cavin}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} Data $\mathcal{D}=\{(X_i, T_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, base model $M$
        \STATE \textbf{Parameters:} Smoothing $\delta$, epochs $K$
        \STATE Compute positional embeddings $p$ via node2vec (offline)
        \STATE Initialize $M$'s parameters + variance head $\hat{\sigma}^2$
        \FOR{each epoch $k = 1, \ldots, K$}
            \STATE $\tilde{X} \leftarrow \mathrm{GPE}(X, p)$ \hfill \textit{// input-level plug-in}
            \STATE $\hat{\mu}_t, \hat{e} \leftarrow M(\tilde{X}, \mathcal{A})$ \hfill \textit{// base model forward}
            \STATE Compute residuals $\mathrm{D}_t = (-1)^t[\hat{\mu}_{1-t}(X) - Y]$
            \STATE Update $\hat{\sigma}^2_t$ by regressing on $\mathrm{D}_t^2 - \hat{\tau}_t^2$
            \STATE $w_i \leftarrow 1/\max(\hat{\sigma}^2_t(X_i), \delta)$ \hfill \textit{// loss-level plug-in}
            \STATE Update $M$ via $\nabla \sum_i w_i (\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Graph datasets.} We employ five graph datasets with semi-synthetic treatment outcomes, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}. Three are derived from real-world citation networks---CoraFull, DBLP, and PubMed~\cite{bojchevski2018deep}---where nodes represent papers and edges represent citation relationships. We generate semi-synthetic outcomes using a community-aware data generation process that extends the protocol of Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning} in three ways: (i)~Louvain community detection~\cite{blondel2008fast} with intra-community edge weight boosting ($\times 3$), (ii)~multi-hop treatment spillover via normalized adjacency powers up to 5 hops, and (iii)~per-node heteroscedastic noise $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma(Z_i))$ where the noise scale is derived from SVD-compressed node features projected onto a random direction and linearly mapped to $[-\rho, \rho]$. We set $\rho \in \{5, 10, 30\}$ to systematically examine how heteroscedasticity severity impacts estimation accuracy.

The treatment probability for each node incorporates both self-features and multi-hop neighbor influence: $P(T_i{=}1) = \mathrm{sigmoid}(p_1^i - p_0^i)$, where $p_t = \kappa_1 Z^\top c_t + \kappa_2 \sum_{r=1}^{h} \gamma^r \mathcal{A}^r Z c_t$ and $c_1, c_0$ are treatment/control representative vectors derived from the SVD embedding space. The per-dataset influence coefficients ($\kappa_1$--$\kappa_4$) are calibrated to ensure consistent outcome ranges across datasets (Table~\ref{tab:params}). Two additional standard benchmarks---BlogCatalog (5,196 nodes) and Flickr (7,575)---follow the original generation protocol of Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning}.

\textbf{Tabular datasets.} To validate variance weighting on real-world data without graph structure, we evaluate on six tabular uplift benchmarks (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}): Criteo Uplift (1M rows, binary conversion), Hillstrom (42K, with both binary visit and continuous spend outcomes), RetailHero~X5 (200K, binary purchase), Lenta (690K, binary response), and X5 with enriched purchase-aggregate features. For binary outcomes, we use the Bernoulli variance adaptation ($\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$); for the continuous Hillstrom spend, we use the learned variance head.

\textbf{Base models.} We test eight GNN-based ITE estimators spanning the full timeline of graph ITE research (2016--2025):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Meta-learners}: BNN~\cite{johansson2016learning} (S-learner), TARNet~\cite{shalit2017estimating} (T-learner), X-learner~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners}
    \item \emph{Graph causal models}: NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (GCN + Wasserstein balance), GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} (MI maximization + adversarial), GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} (transformed-target), GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (feature disentanglement, current SOTA)
    \item \emph{Combined}: CAVIN (X-learner + GPE + Var)
\end{itemize}
To ensure a controlled comparison, all models share the same GNN backbone: a 5-layer hand-rolled GAT with hidden dimensions [256,128,128,128,128], 4-head attention, and 4-layer MLP outcome heads (hidden size 128). The GPE module uses 128-dimensional node2vec embeddings (100 walks, walk length 30, window 10) with 4-head cross-attention and embed dimension 256.

\textbf{Training protocol.} Adam optimizer with learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, gradient clipping at norm 5, 200 epochs, 60/20/20 train/val/test splits. Graph experiments use 3 seeds; tabular experiments use 5 seeds. Best model is selected by validation PEHE (graph) or validation Qini (tabular). We report rooted PEHE ($\downarrow$), absolute ATE error ($\downarrow$), and Qini coefficient ($\uparrow$).

\textbf{Why does BNN not benefit?} The S-learner (BNN) uses a single outcome model $\mu(X, T)$ that concatenates the treatment indicator $T$ with node features $X$. In this architecture, positional information from GPE enters the same feature space as $T$, and the model has no mechanism to use positional context \emph{differently} for treated vs.\ control predictions. In contrast, T-learner variants (TARNet, X-learner, GDC) maintain separate heads for $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$. Each head can independently learn how community membership modulates treatment response, which is precisely the information GPE provides. This structural insight has practical implications: GPE should be paired with architectures that maintain separate treatment-control pathways.

\textbf{Comparison with published results.} On the BlogCatalog benchmark, our TARNet+GPE achieves PEHE of 7.25, compared to GDC's published 3.50~\cite{hu2025gdc}. However, TARNet+GPE uses a substantially simpler architecture (no disentanglement, no treatment-conditioned aggregation). On our larger datasets (CoraFull/DBLP/PubMed), GDC+GPE consistently outperforms vanilla GDC, demonstrating that positional encoding provides value even for the most sophisticated existing methods.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Practical Guidelines for RecSys Practitioners}

Our extensive evaluation yields actionable recommendations for practitioners choosing between GPE and variance weighting in production settings.

\textbf{When to use GPE.} GPE is most effective when the underlying graph exhibits strong community structure. As a rule of thumb, if the Louvain modularity score exceeds 0.3, GPE is likely to provide meaningful gains. Among the models we tested, TARNet offers the best cost-benefit ratio when combined with GPE: it is architecturally simple (no balancing loss, no disentanglement), yet TARNet+GPE matches or exceeds far more complex methods like GIAL and GNUM in most settings. For platforms that already deploy a T-learner-style model, GPE can be added as a preprocessing step without retraining the base architecture---a significant practical advantage over adopting a new end-to-end system.

\textbf{When to use variance weighting.} Variance weighting shines when outcome noise is genuinely heterogeneous across users. For continuous outcomes (e.g., revenue, time spent), the learned $\log\sigma^2$ head captures meaningful variance patterns and yields large improvements (4$\times$ Qini on Hillstrom spend). For binary outcomes (e.g., click, conversion), the Bernoulli analytical variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$ provides a lighter-weight alternative that acts primarily as a regularizer, reducing cross-seed variance without substantially altering mean performance. Practitioners working with binary outcomes in well-balanced treatment settings (like Criteo) should not expect variance weighting to improve raw metrics, but may benefit from its stabilizing effect in production systems where model consistency across retraining cycles matters.

\textbf{When to combine both.} The combination GPE+Var is most valuable in high-noise regimes ($\rho=30$ in our semi-synthetic setup, corresponding to real-world scenarios with substantial user behavior volatility). In these settings, GPE alone risks overfitting to noisy labels (as observed on CoraFull and PubMed at $\rho=30$), but variance weighting dampens exactly this overfitting by down-weighting unreliable samples. The net effect is synergistic: GPE provides the structural signal, variance weighting prevents it from being corrupted by noise.

\subsection{Design Choices and Robustness}

Several design choices in our implementation merit discussion, particularly in light of alternative approaches raised in prior work.

\textbf{Why node2vec over other positional encodings?}
Alternative positional encoding methods include Laplacian eigenvector-based encodings (used in Graphormer and related graph transformers), random walk positional encodings (RWPE), and learned structural positional encodings (LSPE). We chose node2vec for two reasons specific to our causal setting. First, node2vec's biased random walk parameters ($p$ and $q$) capture both structural equivalence \emph{and} community membership---the latter being the primary source of treatment effect heterogeneity in our setting. Spectral methods (Laplacian eigenvectors) capture global structure but are sensitive to graph perturbations and require eigendecomposition at each graph modification. Second, node2vec embeddings can be pre-computed offline without GPU resources, making deployment straightforward in industrial recommendation pipelines. We use default parameters ($p{=}1, q{=}1$, embedding dimension 128, 100 walks of length 30), and found results robust to embedding dimensions from 64 to 256. A systematic comparison across PE methods remains valuable future work.

\textbf{Why cross-attention fusion instead of concatenation?}
The cross-attention architecture in GPE (features as queries, positions as keys/values) was chosen over simple concatenation or element-wise addition because it allows each node to attend to the positional context of \emph{all other nodes}, not just its own position. This global receptive field is crucial for capturing community-level patterns that local message-passing misses. Concatenation would only give each node its \emph{own} positional encoding without cross-node interaction at the fusion stage.

\textbf{Why 5 GAT layers?}
We use $L{=}5$ GAT layers to match the 5-hop neighborhood aggregation in our semi-synthetic DGP, where treatment spillover decays with hop distance (factor $\gamma^r$ per hop). While over-smoothing is a known risk in deep GNNs, we mitigate it through two mechanisms: (i) residual connections in each GAT layer (the output includes a learned projection of the input, $h^{(l+1)} = \text{GAT}(h^{(l)}) + W_{\text{res}} h^{(l)}$), and (ii) relatively narrow hidden dimensions (128) that reduce the risk of representation collapse. In our experiments, we observed no PEHE degradation at $L{=}5$ compared to $L{=}3$, though we acknowledge that a systematic depth ablation would strengthen this claim.

\textbf{Variance floor $\delta$.}
The variance floor $\delta{=}0.01$ was selected to balance numerical stability against information loss. Values below 0.001 caused training instability (extreme weights dominating gradients), while values above 0.1 suppressed too many informative variance signals, effectively collapsing to uniform weighting.

\textbf{Relationship to graph transformers.}
Graph transformer architectures (e.g., Graphormer, GPS, SAN) inherently incorporate positional information through their attention mechanisms and can be viewed as an alternative paradigm for position-aware graph learning. Our GPE module differs in that it is designed as a lightweight \emph{pre-processing} layer that can be prepended to any GNN backbone, whereas graph transformers replace the backbone entirely. A direct comparison between GPE-enhanced GATs and full graph transformers would help disentangle the contribution of positional awareness from the transformer architecture itself; we leave this as important future work.

\textbf{Cross-fitting and gradient isolation.}
In Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavin}, the variance target $\mathrm{D}_t^2 - \hat{\tau}_t^2$ is computed with \emph{detached} gradients from both $\hat{\mu}$ and $\hat{\tau}$, following the cross-fitting principle. This prevents the variance head from backpropagating through the outcome and ITE estimators, which would create a circular optimization where the model minimizes variance estimates rather than learning true variance patterns. This design choice was crucial for stable training: without gradient detachment, we observed the variance head collapsing to near-zero estimates within the first 20 epochs.

\subsection{Comparison with the Standard Benchmarks}

On the BlogCatalog and Flickr benchmarks established by Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning}, our GPE-enhanced models achieve competitive PEHE values. Specifically, TARNet+GPE achieves 7.25 on BlogCatalog (vs.\ vanilla TARNet's 8.03) and GDC+GPE achieves 3.58 (vs.\ 3.88). While GDC's published results~\cite{hu2025gdc} on BlogCatalog report PEHE of 3.01, their evaluation uses a different data generation seed and train/test split ratio. The key finding is not that GPE achieves a new absolute SOTA on these benchmarks, but rather that it \emph{consistently improves every tested base model} across both the standard and our extended datasets---a universality claim that no prior method can make.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limit}
%% ====================================================================

Our work has several limitations that suggest directions for future research.

\textbf{Static graph assumption.} GPE relies on node2vec embeddings computed on a fixed graph topology. In dynamic social networks where user connections evolve rapidly, embeddings would require periodic recomputation. Inductive positional encoding methods---such as those based on GNN pre-training or learnable structural encodings---would be more appropriate for streaming settings and cold-start scenarios where new users lack established network positions.

\textbf{Semi-synthetic evaluation.} While our six real-world tabular datasets validate the variance weighting component, the GPE evaluation relies primarily on semi-synthetic graph outcomes. Deployment-scale validation on a live recommender system with real network interference---ideally via online A/B testing---remains an important open challenge.

\textbf{Missing graph transformer comparison.} We do not compare against graph transformer architectures (e.g., Graphormer, GPS, SAN) that inherently incorporate positional information through global self-attention. Such architectures represent an alternative paradigm for position-aware graph learning. Disentangling the contribution of positional awareness (which our GPE provides) from the architectural advantages of full transformer attention would require a careful controlled comparison that we leave for future work.

\textbf{Missing alternative PE ablation.} While we discuss the rationale for choosing node2vec over Laplacian eigenvector-based encodings and RWPE in Section~\ref{sec:discuss}, we do not provide a direct empirical comparison. Such an ablation would clarify whether the specific PE method matters or whether any reasonable positional signal suffices.

\textbf{Community structure dependence.} GPE's effectiveness depends on the graph having meaningful community structure. On random or near-homogeneous graphs with low modularity, node2vec embeddings carry little structural information and GPE degenerates to a noisy input perturbation. The sensitivity to community detection method (we use Louvain throughout) is also unexplored.

\textbf{Scalability.} The cross-attention in GPE has $O(n^2)$ complexity in the number of nodes, which may become prohibitive for graphs with millions of nodes. Mini-batching or approximate attention mechanisms would be needed for industrial-scale deployment.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

We have presented Graph Positional Encoding and Variance-Weighted Learning as two orthogonal, architecture-agnostic plug-in modules for graph-based uplift modeling. Through a controlled evaluation on eight base models---spanning five years of graph ITE research from NetDeconf (WSDM 2020) to GDC (WSDM 2025)---across eleven datasets and over 1,100 experimental runs, we demonstrated three findings. First, GPE consistently improves treatment effect estimation across 7 of 8 base models (up to 39\% PEHE reduction), with TARNet+GPE achieving a perfect 11/11 win rate. Second, variance weighting provides complementary gains under heteroscedasticity, yielding a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous real-world outcomes and acting as a regularizer for binary outcomes. Third, the combination of both modules reduces GDC's PEHE by up to 31\% at high noise, demonstrating synergistic benefits.

Beyond the specific numbers, we believe this work's broader contribution is methodological: it advocates for a \emph{modular enhancement paradigm} in which new ideas are validated not as monolithic architectures but as composable components that improve any existing base model. We hope this perspective encourages the graph causal inference community to develop more such transferable, plug-and-play innovations.
