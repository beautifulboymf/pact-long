\begin{abstract}
Graph-based uplift modeling powers targeted recommendation on social networks, where mistargeting erodes engagement and wastes promotional budget. Progress has come from increasingly elaborate GNN architectures, but the field lacks a principled account of which error source each design is correcting. We show that a node's latent structural position is a \emph{dual-root confounder}: it simultaneously biases treatment assignment and drives heteroscedastic outcome noise, so the estimation error of any graph ITE model decomposes into a bias term and a variance term sharing this single cause. The decomposition prescribes two targeted, architecture-agnostic corrections---\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}, which injects node2vec embeddings through cross-attention to adjust for the confounder, and \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning}, which reweights the training loss by estimated per-sample noise---both applicable to any base estimator. Over 1{,}100 controlled runs across seven base models (from NetDeconf at WSDM~2020 to GDC at WSDM~2025) and five graph plus five tabular uplift datasets validate the theory: GPE reduces PEHE in 75\% of graph settings (47/63 cells), with gains concentrated in separate-head architectures (TARNet+GPE wins 10/11). Variance weighting yields a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on Hillstrom spend and, under high heteroscedasticity, cuts GDC's PEHE by up to 29\%. A controlled Graphformer backbone comparison confirms the causal model independently of our specific modules.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/Uplift_cover.png}
    \caption{Mutual influence among users in a social network. Users in community~1 (blue) and community~2 (green) exhibit different treatment response patterns. The orange user is the estimation target. Grey shading indicates higher outcome noise (heteroscedasticity).}
    \label{fig:cover}
\end{figure}

Targeted interventions are only as valuable as the models that predict who will respond to them. Digital platforms collectively spend hundreds of billions of dollars annually on personalized incentives~\cite{goldenberg2020free, parshakov2020spillover}, and a misallocated promotion is not merely wasteful---it degrades user experience and the signal-quality of future recommendations. The core modeling task is estimating the Individual Treatment Effect (ITE): distinguishing users who genuinely change behavior under treatment from those who would have acted identically regardless. On social platforms, this task acquires an extra dimension through network interference: a user's response is shaped not only by their own features but by the concurrent actions of their social connections~\cite{aral2011creating}. Users in tightly-knit communities respond up to 2.3$\times$ more strongly to identical promotions than socially isolated users~\cite{luo2021behavioural, DEVALCK2009185}---a gap that i.i.d.\ estimators~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards, shalit2017estimating} cannot recover. Figure~\ref{fig:cover} illustrates the phenomenon: community membership and outcome noise are both spatially structured in real social networks.

A growing line of GNN-based ITE methods has responded to this challenge---NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM~2020), GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph}, GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum}, and the current SOTA GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025)---yet every entry in the sequence introduces a monolithic architecture whose improvements cannot compose with one another. More importantly, the field advances without a principled account of \emph{which} error source graph information is correcting: is there one, or several? Evaluation compounds the issue. Real-world graph uplift data at recommendation scale is effectively unavailable, so the literature has consolidated around two semi-synthetic benchmarks---BlogCatalog and Flickr---that graft simulated treatments and outcomes onto real graphs. The combination of monolithic designs and a thin, semi-synthetic benchmark suite leaves the community unable to decide which model components matter for which reasons.

Our primary contribution is diagnostic and theoretical. We identify and formally characterize a node's latent structural position $Z^*$---its community membership, centrality, and bridging role---as a \emph{dual-root confounder}: $Z^*$ simultaneously acts along (1)~$Z^* \to T$ (position influences treatment assignment), (2)~$Z^* \to Y(t)$ (position influences potential outcomes), and (3)~$Z^* \to \sigma^2(Y)$ (position drives outcome-noise variance). The first two paths constitute classical confounding; the third drives heteroscedasticity. We derive a PEHE decomposition showing that the estimation error of \emph{any} graph ITE model splits into a bias term (from paths 1--2) and a variance term (from path 3) that share $Z^*$ as their single unobserved cause. Prior graph ITE methods implicitly attack symptoms of one path or another; none name, let alone address, the shared root. Once $Z^*$ is named, the decomposition prescribes two targeted corrections, each attacking a different term while composing with any base estimator.

\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)} approximates $Z^*$ through node2vec embeddings and fuses them into an existing backbone via cross-attention, implementing Veitch et al.'s proxy-confounder adjustment~\cite{veitch2019using} to reduce the bias term. \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning} estimates per-sample noise via a $\log\sigma^2$ head and reweights the training loss following Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936} to reduce the variance term. GPE operates at the \emph{input} level and variance weighting at the \emph{loss} level, so neither touches the base model's internals; both are plug-in modules that compose with any graph ITE backbone. The prescriptions are complementary in a way the theory predicts and the experiments confirm: at high noise, GPE alone can amplify position-correlated variance, while the combination addresses both error components.

Because large-scale real-world graph uplift data does not exist, we validate the theory through a controlled plug-in protocol on seven base models---spanning NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} to GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc}---over five graph datasets, including re-processed releases of BlogCatalog and Flickr that correct labeling and sparsity issues we observed in prior versions. We additionally test Variance-Weighted Learning on five real-world tabular uplift benchmarks: because the variance half of the dual-root theory follows from any position-dependent noise source rather than from graph topology per se, tabular experiments demonstrate that this prescription generalizes to production recommendation regimes where graph ITE data is unavailable, and rule out graph-specific artifacts as the driver of the observed gains. Over 1{,}100 controlled runs support the theory: GPE reduces PEHE in 87\% of separate-head graph settings, the combined correction cuts GDC's error by up to 29\% at high noise, and variance weighting yields a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous tabular outcomes (Hillstrom spend). Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Theoretical.} We prove that network position is a dual-root confounder and derive a PEHE decomposition that unifies bias and variance under a single latent cause (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
    \item \textbf{Methodological.} We propose two corrections prescribed directly by the decomposition---GPE (input-level, bias) and Variance-Weighted Learning (loss-level, variance)---that compose with any base estimator (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Empirical.} Across 1{,}100+ runs on seven base models and ten datasets (five graph, five tabular), we validate the theory, and release cleaned versions of BlogCatalog and Flickr together with code at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PACT}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{ITE estimation and uplift modeling.}
Traditional treatment-effect estimation spans tree-based methods (uplift trees~\cite{radcliffe2011real}, causal forests~\cite{athey2016recursive}) and meta-learner frameworks (S-, T-, and X-learners~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards}). Deep variants extend these through representation learning: BNN~\cite{johansson2016learning} employs a shared backbone in the S-learner style, TARNet/CFRNet~\cite{shalit2017estimating} maintains separate treatment-control heads under a T-learner, and SNet~\cite{curth2021nonparametric} disentangles shared and treatment-specific components. In recommendation, E3IR~\cite{sun2024e3ir} couples uplift with budget allocation and CDUM~\cite{meng2025cdum} optimizes real-time treatment decisions, but neither uses graph structure. The theoretical foundation most relevant to our Variance-Weighted Learning module is the R-Learner~\cite{nie2021rlearner}: reformulating CATE estimation as a residual-on-residual regression and weighting each sample by the inverse of its target's conditional variance yields quasi-oracle convergence rates. This is the Gauss--Markov--style efficiency argument we import---as a loss-level plug-in that attaches to any of the base models above (S-, T-, or X-learner; BNN, TARNet, SNet; and the graph models below), not as a replacement for any of them. All of these methods assume i.i.d.\ samples and implicitly treat estimation bias and outcome-noise heterogeneity as independent error sources---an assumption we will show is untenable on social platforms, where both error components trace to a node's position in the network.

\textbf{Causal inference under network interference.}
A focused line of GNN-based ITE estimators addresses the i.i.d.\ violation above. NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM~2020) pioneered GCN-based deconfounding with Wasserstein representation balancing. GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} added mutual-information maximization and adversarial balance; GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} introduced transformed-target estimation; GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025, current SOTA) disentangles confounder, instrumental, and adjustment features. CauGramer~\cite{wu2025caugramer} (ICLR~2025) proposes a full causal graph Transformer with minimax moment constraints, requiring complete architecture replacement. Each work introduces a monolithic design whose innovations do not transfer to other models; more fundamentally, none of these papers names \emph{which} error component its structural inductive bias is actually correcting. We argue that all of them are attacking partial symptoms of a single latent cause---a node's structural position, which simultaneously confounds treatment assignment and drives heteroscedastic outcome noise---and that naming this cause admits modular corrections where the field currently has only monolithic ones.

\textbf{Positional and structural encoding in GNNs.}
Standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish structurally equivalent nodes that occupy different global positions~\cite{velickovic2017graph}, motivating a rich positional-encoding (PE) literature. Absolute encodings inject topological statistics into attention: Graphormer~\cite{ying2021graphormer} uses centrality, spatial distance, and edge features. Spectral encodings use Laplacian eigenvectors: SAN~\cite{kreuzer2021san} reweights attention by eigenvalues, while SignNet~\cite{lim2023signnet} handles the sign ambiguity inherent in spectral PE. Hybrid recipes such as GraphGPS~\cite{rampasek2022graphgps} combine PE with local message-passing and global attention. In recommendation, PGTR~\cite{chen2025pgtr} augments LightGCN with spectral, degree, and PageRank encodings for collaborative filtering. Random-walk embeddings such as node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} offer a lightweight, offline-precomputable alternative that captures both structural equivalence and community membership. None of this work was developed for causal ITE, and none studies positional information as a proxy for an unobserved structural confounder---the role that our GPE plays by fusing node2vec embeddings into \emph{existing} causal GNN backbones rather than replacing them.

\textbf{Heteroscedastic modeling in deep learning and ITE.}
Modeling input-dependent noise variance in neural networks dates to Nix \& Weigend~(1994) and was revisited in the multi-task uncertainty weighting framework of Kendall \& Gal~\cite{kendall2018multi}. Within the ITE literature, variance-weighted estimators have been explored for tabular settings~\cite{SHI2024111679, mo2022efficient, abrevaya2023estimation}. This line of work treats heteroscedasticity as a generic modeling concern independent of confounding; it does not connect variance structure to structural causes, and it has not been adapted to graph-based ITE or to pseudo-outcome regression. Two practical challenges distinguish our setting: the regression target (pseudo-effect $D_i$) is itself a noisy estimate rather than a direct observation, and binary outcomes admit a qualitatively different variance structure. We address both, via a $\log\sigma^2$ parameterization with gradient-isolated cross-fitting for continuous outcomes and an analytical Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$ for binary outcomes.

\textbf{Positioning.} We bridge these four lines by identifying network position as a \emph{dual-root confounder} and deriving two modular, architecture-agnostic corrections prescribed by the PEHE decomposition it induces. To our knowledge, this is the first controlled plug-in evaluation in graph-based ITE estimation, extending the plug-and-play paradigm that has proven valuable elsewhere in graph recommendation~\cite{zheng2025lighterx}.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Graph ITE and Pseudo-Outcome Regression}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ be a graph with adjacency matrix $\mathcal{A}$. Each node $i$ carries features $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, a binary treatment $T_i \in \{0,1\}$, and an observed outcome $Y_i$; we write $X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$ for aggregated features over multi-hop neighbors $\mathcal{N}_i^{1,\ldots,r}$. The dataset is $\mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, T_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$ paired with $\mathcal{A}$. Because outcomes depend on peers, SUTVA is replaced by three standard assumptions on networked data~\cite{guo2020learning}:

\begin{assumption}[Network Consistency]
$Y_i = Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i)$, where $\mathcal{T}_i = \mathrm{agg}(T_{\mathcal{N}_i^{1,\ldots,r}})$ aggregates neighbors' treatments.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Unconfoundedness]
$Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i) \perp (T_i, T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Overlap]
$0 < p(T_i, T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i} \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) < 1$.
\end{assumption}

The estimand is the Network CATE $\tau(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}]$. Since both potential outcomes are never jointly observed, we adopt the standard pseudo-outcome reduction of Künzel et al.~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners} (the X-learner): regress against an imputed target $\tilde{D}$ satisfying $\mathbb{E}[\tilde{D} \mid X] = \tau(X)$, which makes minimizing $\mathbb{E}[(\tilde{D} - \hat{\tau})^2]$ equivalent to minimizing the oracle risk $\mathbb{E}[(\tau - \hat{\tau})^2]$. In practice we fit outcome models $\mu_t = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid T{=}t, X, X_{\mathcal{N}}]$ for $t \in \{0,1\}$, form cross-residuals $\mathrm{D}_1 = Y(1) - \mu_0$ and $\mathrm{D}_0 = \mu_1 - Y(0)$, and combine them via propensity-weighted regression. This construction is an off-the-shelf tool, not a contribution of this work; our contribution is the dual-root identification of network position as a shared cause of the bias and variance terms that any such pseudo-outcome regressor incurs. Every symbol introduced here---$X_i, T_i, Y_i, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{N}, \mu_t, \tilde{D}$---is used without redefinition in the method section.

\subsection{PEHE Decomposition and the Dual-Root Structure}\label{sec:theory}

Let $Z^* \in \mathcal{Z}$ denote the latent structural position of a node---a low-dimensional summary of community membership, centrality, and bridging role that is not observed but is partially recoverable from the graph topology. We posit three simultaneous causal paths from $Z^*$: (i)~$Z^* \to T$ (position shifts treatment assignment), (ii)~$Z^* \to Y(t)$ (position shifts potential outcomes), and (iii)~$Z^* \to \sigma^2(Y)$ (position drives outcome-noise variance). Paths (i)--(ii) together constitute classical confounding; path (iii) drives heteroscedasticity. Network unconfoundedness (Assumption~2) conditions only on observed $(X, X_{\mathcal{N}})$, but $Z^*$ is \emph{residual}---the structural information not captured by raw features---so both effects persist in any estimator trained on $X$ and $X_{\mathcal{N}}$ alone.

Under this setup the squared PEHE admits the standard bias--variance decomposition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pehe_decomp}
    \mathrm{PEHE}^2 = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat{\tau}(X) - \bar{\tau}(X))^2\right]}_{\text{bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{D} \mid X]\right]}_{\text{variance}},
\end{equation}
with $\bar{\tau}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\tau \mid X]$ the best estimator given $X$. The bias term is inflated whenever $Z^*$ confounds $T \leftarrow Z^* \to Y(t)$; the variance term is inflated whenever $\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{D} \mid X] = \sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is position-dependent. The variance term further absorbs first-stage estimation error from $\hat{\mu}_0, \hat{\mu}_1$, which we control with gradient-detached cross-fitting (Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact}). Because the two terms are orthogonal error sources, they call for orthogonal corrections.

\begin{proposition}[Network Position as Dual Error Root]\label{prop:dualroot}
Under network unconfoundedness conditional on $(X, X_{\mathcal{N}}, Z^*)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{Proxy reduces bias.} If $p(Z^*)$ satisfies Veitch et al.'s network unconfoundedness condition~\cite{veitch2019using}---i.e., $Y(t) \perp T \mid X, X_{\mathcal{N}}, p(Z^*)$---then conditioning on $p(Z^*)$ eliminates the $Z^*$-induced confounding bias in the PEHE bias term. Bias reduction is monotone in proxy quality: a better approximation of $Z^*$ yields a smaller residual bias.

    \item \textbf{Inverse-variance weighting reduces variance.} When $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon \mid X] = \sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is position-dependent, the feasible GLS estimator $\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{WLS}} = \arg\min \sum_i w_i (D_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$ with $w_i = 1/\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i)$ achieves $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{WLS}}) \preceq \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ in the Löwner order, directly reducing the variance term in Eq.~\ref{eq:pehe_decomp}.

    \item \textbf{Complementarity at high noise.} The two corrections target orthogonal components of $\mathrm{PEHE}^2$. However, without variance correction, bias correction alone can be counterproductive when $\sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is large: the proxy $p(Z^*)$ amplifies the position-dependent signal in pseudo-labels, but since that same position also drives noise, the model overfits to noisy pseudo-labels. Only the combination simultaneously reduces both components.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot} is a synthesis of three existing results---Veitch et al.'s proxy-confounder theorem~\cite{veitch2019using}, Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936}, and the bias--variance decomposition of PEHE~\cite{shalit2017estimating}---applied jointly to the graph ITE setting. Two caveats on part~(b) are worth making explicit. First, the Löwner-order guarantee is stated for the linear/GLS estimator; for the nonlinear neural networks used in our base models, the same inverse-variance--weighted objective retains its meaning as minimizing a conditional risk reweighted by the inverse noise variance, and the R-learner~\cite{nie2021rlearner} provides theoretical support for variance-weighted pseudo-outcome regression in the nonlinear regime, though our module is not a literal R-learner implementation (see the Theoretical Motivation remark in Section~\ref{sec:var}). Second, the result is an oracle statement with known $\sigma^2(X, Z^*)$; in practice we substitute a $\log\sigma^2$ head with gradient-detached cross-fitting (Section~\ref{sec:method}), which yields consistent variance estimates and prevents first-stage error from inflating the weights. Its empirical content is that, when tied to a single latent cause $Z^*$, these three results predict the exact pattern we observe in Section~\ref{sec:exp}: GPE alone helps at low noise, is counterproductive in isolation at high noise, and the combination dominates throughout. Part~(a) does not require node2vec specifically: any function $p(\cdot)$ of the graph topology that approximates $Z^*$ reduces the bias term---a claim we verify against a Graphformer backbone in Section~\ref{sec:discuss}, where degree-centrality encoding acts as a coarser proxy and makes adding GPE on top redundant.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

We present two plug-in modules. GPE operates at the \emph{input} level; variance weighting operates at the \emph{loss} level. Neither modifies the internal architecture of any base model.

\subsection{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}\label{sec:gpe}

Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a) establishes that any proxy for the latent position $Z^*$ reduces confounding bias. GPE implements this proxy via node2vec embeddings fused through cross-attention. Standard GNNs aggregate local neighborhoods but lack explicit awareness of a node's global position. Two nodes with isomorphic local structures may belong to different communities with distinct treatment response patterns. GPE addresses this by fusing raw features $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ with pre-computed node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$ via multi-head cross-attention, where features serve as queries and positional embeddings as keys and values:
\begin{align}
    Q &= hW_Q,\; K = pW_K,\; V = pW_V, \label{eq:qkv} \\
    \mathrm{head}_k &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(Q_k K_k^\top / \sqrt{d_k}\right) V_k, \label{eq:head}\\
    z &= \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl([h;\; W_o\,\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_H)] + f_{\mathrm{res}}([h;p])\bigr), \label{eq:fuse}
\end{align}
where $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_o$ are learnable projections, LN denotes layer normalization, $[\cdot;\cdot]$ is concatenation, and $f_{\mathrm{res}}$ is a residual projection. The output $z$ replaces $h$ as the input to any downstream GNN backbone.

\textbf{Plug-in interface.} For any base model $M$ that takes node features $X$ as input, the GPE-enhanced version is simply $M_{\text{+GPE}}(\cdot) = M(\mathrm{GPE}(X, p), \cdot)$. The node2vec embeddings are computed once offline and remain fixed during training, adding negligible runtime overhead.

\subsection{Variance-Weighted Learning}\label{sec:var}

Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(b) establishes that inverse-variance weighting reduces the variance component of PEHE when outcome noise is position-dependent. Standard meta-learners treat all samples equally, implicitly assuming homoscedastic noise. User behavior volatility is far from uniform---some users exhibit highly consistent responses while others are erratic. We model the observed pseudo-treatment effect as $D_i = \tau(X_i) + \varepsilon_i$, where $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_i \mid X_i] = \sigma^2(X_i)$ is feature-dependent.

We estimate $\sigma^2(X_i)$ via the identity $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[D^2 \mid X] - \tau^2(X)$, training a dedicated variance head with the regression target $\log((\mathrm{D} - \hat{\tau})^2 + \epsilon)$, where $\hat{\tau}$ is a pilot estimate from the outcome heads with gradients detached (cross-fitting). The head predicts $\log \sigma^2$ directly---not $\sigma^2$---with the true variance recovered via exponentiation. This log-parameterization guarantees strict positivity and avoids the degeneracy where unconstrained heads collapse to zero; it also matches the logarithmic scale of the regression target. The sample weights are:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\max\{\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i), \delta\}}, \quad \delta > 0,
\end{equation}
and the refined ITE estimator solves $\hat{\tau} = \arg\min_{\hat{\tau}'} \sum_{i} w_i (\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}'(X_i))^2$.

\textbf{Binary outcome adaptation.} For binary outcomes (e.g., click/conversion in recommendation), the noise variance is analytically determined by the Bernoulli model: $\sigma^2_t(X_i) = \mu_t(X_i)(1 - \mu_t(X_i))$, requiring no learned variance head.

\begin{remark}[Predicted Interaction]\label{rem:interaction}
Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(c) predicts that at high noise ($\rho \geq 30$), GPE alone can be counterproductive: the proxy $p(Z^*)$ amplifies the position-dependent signal, but the same position also drives the noise $\sigma^2(Z^*)$. The model overfits to noisy pseudo-labels. Only the combination of GPE (bias reduction) and variance weighting (noise suppression) addresses both error sources simultaneously. We validate this prediction in Section~\ref{sec:var_exp}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Theoretical Motivation]
The Gauss--Markov theorem~\cite{Aitken_1936} guarantees that WLS achieves $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{WLS}}) \preceq \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ in the Löwner order for linear models with known variance. Our setting is nonlinear and uses \emph{estimated} variance, so this guarantee does not apply formally. We invoke the result as motivation under two interpretations: (i) the last layer of any network is a linear regressor on fixed features $\phi(X)$, to which Gauss--Markov applies conditionally; (ii) $\tau(X)$ is locally linear by Taylor's theorem. Closing the gap between theory and practice for plug-in WLS with estimated weights in deep networks is an open question; Section~\ref{sec:exp} provides the empirical justification.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{images/Uplift.png}
    \caption{Overview. The left two panels depict representative existing learners that ignore graph structure or heteroscedastic noise (S/T-Learner; the X-Learner is shown with its standard static propensity weights). The right panel shows the two plug-in modules contributed in this paper---GPE (input level, bias correction) and variance-weighted loss (loss level, variance correction)---which attach to any graph ITE backbone without modifying its internal architecture. For illustration we overlay the plug-ins on an X-learner base; the same attachment applies to BNN, TARNet, NetDeconf, GIAL, GNUM, and GDC, as evaluated in Table~\ref{tab:main}.}
    \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure*}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}, the two plug-ins attach to any base model $M$ without modifying its internals. First, node2vec embeddings are fused with raw features via GPE (Eqs.~\ref{eq:qkv}--\ref{eq:fuse}); the fused features replace $M$'s input. Second, $M$'s existing outcome loss is reweighted by inverse variance estimates from a separately trained $\log\sigma^2$ head. The base model $M$ is interchangeable: we plug these modules into seven different backbones in Section~\ref{sec:exp}. The complete training procedure is formalized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
    \caption{Plug-in Training Procedure}
    \label{alg:pact}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} Data $\mathcal{D}=\{(X_i, T_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, base model $M$
        \STATE \textbf{Parameters:} Smoothing $\delta$, epochs $K$
        \STATE Compute positional embeddings $p$ via node2vec (offline)
        \STATE Initialize $M$'s parameters + variance head $\hat{\sigma}^2$
        \FOR{each epoch $k = 1, \ldots, K$}
            \STATE $\tilde{X} \leftarrow \mathrm{GPE}(X, p)$ \hfill \textit{// input-level plug-in}
            \STATE $\hat{\mu}_t, \hat{e} \leftarrow M(\tilde{X}, \mathcal{A})$ \hfill \textit{// base model forward}
            \STATE Compute residuals $\mathrm{D}_t = (-1)^t[\hat{\mu}_{1-t}(X) - Y]$
            \STATE Update $\hat{\sigma}^2_t$ by regressing on $\log((\mathrm{D}_t - \hat{\tau}_t)^2 + \epsilon)$ \hfill \textit{// $\hat{\tau}_t$ detached}
            \STATE $w_i \leftarrow 1/\max(\hat{\sigma}^2_t(X_i), \delta)$ \hfill \textit{// loss-level plug-in}
            \STATE Update $M$ via $\nabla \sum_i w_i (\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Graph datasets.} We employ five graph datasets with semi-synthetic treatment outcomes, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}. Three are derived from real-world citation networks---CoraFull, DBLP, and PubMed~\cite{bojchevski2018deep}---where nodes represent papers and edges represent citation relationships. We generate semi-synthetic outcomes using a community-aware data generation process that extends the protocol of Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning} in three ways: (i)~Louvain community detection~\cite{blondel2008fast} with intra-community edge weight boosting ($\times 3$), (ii)~multi-hop treatment spillover via normalized adjacency powers up to 5 hops, and (iii)~per-node heteroscedastic noise $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma(Z_i))$ where the noise scale is derived from SVD-compressed node features projected onto a random direction and linearly mapped to $[-\rho, \rho]$. We set $\rho \in \{5, 10, 30\}$ to systematically examine how heteroscedasticity severity impacts estimation accuracy.

The treatment probability for each node incorporates both self-features and multi-hop neighbor influence: $P(T_i{=}1) = \mathrm{sigmoid}(p_1^i - p_0^i)$, where $p_t = \kappa_1 Z^\top c_t + \kappa_2 \sum_{r=1}^{h} \gamma^r \mathcal{A}^r Z c_t$ and $c_1, c_0$ are treatment/control representative vectors derived from the SVD embedding space. DGP parameters are calibrated per dataset to ensure consistent outcome ranges (full specification in supplementary materials).\footnote{Shared parameters: $C{=}5$, $\beta_0{=}{-}0.3$, $\beta_1{=}0.3$, $\gamma{=}0.6$, hops${=}5$.} We acknowledge that our semi-synthetic DGP injects community structure that may inherently favor community-aware methods like GPE; we mitigate this concern by also evaluating on the independently-generated BlogCatalog and Flickr benchmarks from prior work. These two standard benchmarks---BlogCatalog (5,196 nodes) and Flickr (7,575)---follow the original generation protocol of Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning}.

\textbf{Why does BNN not benefit?} BNN is an S-learner: a single model $\mu(X, T)$ concatenates the treatment indicator with node features. When GPE augments $X$, the positional signal enters the \emph{same} representation as $T$, giving the model no mechanism to condition on community position differently for treated and control predictions. T-learner variants (TARNet, X-learner, GDC) maintain separate heads for $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$, so each head can independently learn how community membership modulates the treatment-specific response---which is exactly the information GPE provides. The practical implication is direct: GPE should be paired with architectures that maintain separate treatment-control prediction pathways.

\textbf{Comparison with BlogCatalog/Flickr benchmarks.} On the independently-generated WSDM benchmarks (BlogCatalog, Flickr~\cite{guo2020learning}), GPE-enhanced models improve over vanilla on the majority of models tested: TARNet+GPE achieves 8.05 versus 9.04 on BlogCatalog and 11.40 versus 11.75 on Flickr. NetDeconf and GIAL show 0/2 win rates on these benchmarks despite 8/9 on our DGP---both employ explicit representation balancing (Wasserstein and adversarial respectively), and on lower-modularity graphs the proxy signal can conflict with the balancing objective rather than complement it. This cross-DGP consistency matters: the WSDM DGP contains no explicit community bias, no Louvain detection, and no intra-community edge boosting, yet GPE still improves the majority of models, ruling out DGP circularity as the primary explanation.

Across all 54 non-BNN cells, the aggregate improvement is highly significant (Wilcoxon signed-rank test $p < 10^{-5}$; sign test $p < 10^{-5}$), confirming that GPE's benefit is a consistent broad pattern rather than a few strong outliers. Individual cells often do not reach significance with 5 seeds, reflecting modest per-cell effect sizes.

\subsection{Why Does GPE Work? A Mechanism Analysis}\label{sec:mechanism}

A natural question is \emph{how} GPE improves ITE estimation. We test three competing hypotheses through targeted experiments on DBLP and CoraFull ($\rho{=}10$, 5 seeds).

\textbf{H1: Confounding debiasing.} Network position is an unobserved confounder: in both our DGP (where community bias $c_b$ enters treatment probability and outcomes) and the WSDM DGP~\cite{guo2020learning} (where the neighbor aggregate $\mathcal{A}Z c_1$ affects both), structural position causally influences treatment assignment and outcomes simultaneously. This is precisely the setting where Veitch et al.~\cite{veitch2019using} showed that network embeddings can serve as valid proxy confounders. GPE makes this confounding signal explicitly available.

We test H1 by measuring the propensity head's AUC with and without GPE (Table~\ref{tab:propensity}). GPE improves propensity estimation in 5 of 6 model-dataset pairs, with the largest gain on GDC/CoraFull (+0.138 AUC). This confirms that GPE helps the model better identify \emph{who} gets treated and \emph{why}---the hallmark of confounding adjustment. To validate that better confounding adjustment translates to better ITE estimation, we computed the rank correlation between $\Delta$AUC and $\Delta$PEHE across all 6 model-dataset pairs. The Spearman rank correlation is $r_s = 0.83$ ($p = 0.04$, $n = 6$). We note the small sample size limits statistical power; this correlation is suggestive rather than definitive. Nevertheless, the direction is consistent across all pairs where GPE improves propensity AUC, and the two largest propensity gains (GDC/CoraFull $+0.138$, GDC/DBLP $+0.077$) correspond to the two largest PEHE reductions.

\textbf{H2: Representation balancing---rejected.} One might expect GPE to reduce the distributional gap between treated and control representations, similar to Wasserstein regularization~\cite{guo2020learning} or adversarial balancing~\cite{chu2021graph}. We measured the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between $T{=}1$ and $T{=}0$ representations and found the opposite: GPE \emph{increases} MMD in all 6 comparisons (ratios 1.12--1.49$\times$). GPE does not work through representation balancing; it preserves treatment-group differences while making them more informative for outcome prediction. This distinguishes our approach from the entire prior line of balancing-based methods.

\textbf{H3: Positional signal vs.\ attention architecture.} To isolate whether the cross-attention design matters or whether simply adding positional features suffices, we compared three TARNet variants on DBLP: (a)~vanilla (no position), (b)~concatenating node2vec to features, and (c)~full GPE cross-attention. Concatenation achieves PEHE 2.10$^{\pm.10}$, slightly outperforming GPE attention (2.32$^{\pm.02}$), while vanilla scores 2.65$^{\pm.14}$. (These H3 runs use a separate hyperparameter sweep; slight differences from Table~\ref{tab:main} reflect this.) The positional signal itself is the primary driver of improvement; cross-attention trades a small PEHE premium for 4$\times$ lower variance across seeds---a valuable stability property for production systems.

\textbf{Unified mechanism.} GPE works through \emph{confounding debiasing}: node2vec embeddings encode the structural position that acts as an unobserved confounder in networked treatment settings. This explains both (i)~why GPE helps on our DGP (explicit community confounders) \emph{and} on the WSDM benchmarks (implicit structural confounders via neighbor aggregation $\mathcal{A}Z c_1$), and (ii)~why BNN fails to benefit (its shared $\mu(X,T)$ head cannot use position to differentially condition treatment-specific predictions). The mechanism is distinct from representation balancing and does not require the attention architecture---any method of injecting positional information would help, though cross-attention adds stability.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}

\subsection{Practical Guidelines for RecSys Practitioners}

The dual-root perspective translates into concrete deployment heuristics.

\textbf{When to use GPE.} GPE is most effective when the social graph has meaningful community structure (Louvain modularity $> 0.3$ is a reasonable threshold) or when treatment assignment correlates with network topology. Among the architectures tested, TARNet offers the best cost-to-benefit ratio: it is simple and lacks any built-in structural mechanism, leaving the most headroom for GPE. Crucially, GPE is a pure preprocessing step---it can be bolted onto any T-learner deployed in production \emph{without} retraining the backbone, which is a significant advantage over full architectural overhauls.

\textbf{When to use variance weighting.} The learned $\log\sigma^2$ head earns its keep on continuous outcomes with genuine noise heterogeneity (revenue, dwell time, spend). For binary outcomes, the analytical Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$ is a lightweight substitute that primarily regularizes training stability rather than shifting mean performance; do not expect it to move Qini on balanced datasets like Criteo.

\textbf{When to combine both.} The dual-root analysis predicts that combining both modules is most valuable when \emph{both} network confounding and outcome noise are substantial---precisely the high-noise, community-structured settings common in social recommendation. Under such conditions, GPE alone risks overfitting to noisy pseudo-labels (a failure mode we observe empirically), while variance weighting alone cannot correct the structural confounding bias. Only the combination addresses both failure modes. The exact configuration (GPE only, Var only, or both) should be decided by the base architecture and noise regime: Table~\ref{tab:var} shows that the optimal combination is dataset-dependent.

\subsection{Design Choices and Robustness}

\textbf{PE method.} On DBLP ($\rho{=}10$, 5~seeds), node2vec ($2.37^{\pm.10}$), Laplacian eigenvectors ($2.27^{\pm.16}$), and degree$+$PageRank ($2.37^{\pm.36}$) all improve over no-PE (PEHE $2.47$), confirming Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a): the positional signal matters more than the specific encoding method. Full results across PE variants are in the supplementary materials.

\textbf{Concatenation vs.\ cross-attention.} Concatenation achieves slightly lower mean PEHE ($2.10$ vs.\ $2.32$, Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}), while cross-attention reduces cross-seed variance by $4{\times}$. We recommend concatenation at scale for its $O(n)$ complexity; cross-attention is preferred when training stability is the priority.

\textbf{Relationship to graph transformers.} To test whether GPE adds value when the backbone already encodes position, we replaced the GAT backbone with a sparse Graphormer-style transformer (learned degree centrality encoding) and evaluated TARNet on DBLP ($\rho{=}10$, 5~seeds). The position-aware backbone alone ($1.95^{\pm.13}$) outperforms GAT ($2.43^{\pm.24}$), confirming that native positional encoding improves causal estimation. GPE substantially improves GAT ($2.43 \to 2.20$, $-9\%$) but \emph{does not} improve the position-aware backbone ($1.95 \to 2.06$). GPE's value is specific to position-unaware GNN architectures; when the backbone already captures position natively, GPE's signal is redundant. This result independently validates our causal model: any proxy for $Z^*$ helps, whether injected externally (GPE) or learned internally (Graphormer-style attention).

A no-community DGP ablation ($\kappa_4{=}0$, no edge boosting) shows GPE improves 4/6 settings even without explicit community structure, confirming that node2vec captures structural roles (hub vs.\ peripheral, bridge vs.\ cluster member) beyond community membership (see supplementary materials for full results).

\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limit}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{Static graph assumption.} Node2vec embeddings are computed once on a fixed topology. Dynamic social networks require periodic recomputation; new users with no established connections expose a cold-start gap that inductive or GNN-pretrained structural encodings would address more naturally.

\textbf{Semi-synthetic graph evaluation.} The tabular benchmarks provide real-world signal for variance weighting, but GPE is validated primarily on semi-synthetic graph outcomes. Deployment-scale evaluation on a live system---ideally via an online A/B test with real network interference---would substantially strengthen the practical case.

\textbf{Limited graph transformer comparison.} Our controlled Graphformer comparison (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}) shows that GPE does \emph{not} improve a position-aware backbone ($1.95 \to 2.06$), confirming that GPE's value is specific to position-unaware GNN architectures. This result is limited to one dataset (DBLP) and one base model (TARNet); broader comparison with full-scale Graphormer, GPS, or SAN architectures would clarify whether this boundary holds universally.

\textbf{PE method scope.} Our PE comparison (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}) shows that node2vec, Laplacian eigenvectors, and degree$+$PageRank all improve ITE estimation comparably, confirming that the positional signal matters more than the encoding method. However, this comparison covers only one dataset and one base model; broader evaluation across datasets and learned PE methods (LSPE, SignNet) would strengthen this finding.

\textbf{Community structure dependence.} Our no-community DGP ablation shows GPE helps even without explicit community structure (4/6 wins; full results in supplementary materials), but the gains are smaller than on the standard DGP. On near-homogeneous or random graphs with very low modularity, GPE may become a noisy input perturbation. Sensitivity to the community detection algorithm (we use Louvain throughout) is also uncharacterized.

\textbf{Scalability.} The cross-attention in GPE has $O(n^2)$ node complexity. For production systems, we recommend defaulting to concatenation fusion (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}), which is $O(n)$ and empirically achieves comparable mean PEHE (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}, H3). Mini-batching or linear-attention approximations are alternatives for settings where the cross-attention's stability benefits are desired at scale.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

The prevailing paradigm in graph-based uplift modeling---each paper proposes a new monolithic architecture, which then supersedes rather than compounds with prior work---limits cumulative progress. We have argued, and empirically demonstrated across seven base models and over 1,100 runs, that modular plug-ins are a viable alternative. GPE improves treatment effect estimation in 87\% of settings across six separate-head architectures (up to 35\% PEHE reduction on TARNet/CoraFull; 10/11 win rate for TARNet). Variance-weighted learning provides orthogonal gains under heteroscedasticity, yielding a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous outcomes (Hillstrom spend) and stabilizing binary-outcome training. Combined, they reduce GDC's PEHE by up to 29\% at high noise without modifying a single layer of its architecture.

The deeper contribution is the approach itself. Both modules operate at the interface of an existing model---input features and training loss---rather than inside it. This design principle makes them immediately applicable to any future architecture in the graph ITE literature, and we hope it prompts the community to ask, more often, whether a new idea improves \emph{everyone}'s model rather than just the one introduced in the same paper.
