\begin{abstract}
Graph-based uplift modeling powers targeted recommendation on social networks, where mistargeting erodes engagement and wastes promotional budget. Progress has come from increasingly elaborate GNN architectures, but the field lacks a principled account of which error source each design is correcting. We show that a node's latent structural position is a \emph{dual-root confounder}: it simultaneously biases treatment assignment and drives heteroscedastic outcome noise, so the estimation error of any graph ITE model decomposes into a bias term and a variance term sharing this single cause. The decomposition prescribes two targeted, architecture-agnostic corrections---\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}, which injects node2vec embeddings through cross-attention to adjust for the confounder, and \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning}, which reweights the training loss by estimated per-sample noise---both applicable to any base estimator. Over 1{,}100 controlled runs across seven base models (from NetDeconf at WSDM~2020 to GDC at WSDM~2025) and five graph plus five tabular uplift datasets validate the theory: GPE reduces PEHE by a mean $7.6\%$ across the six separate-head backbones on 90 (dataset, $\rho$) cells (Wilcoxon $p<10^{-5}$), with per-backbone gains up to $-9.6\%$ (NetDeconf) and up to $-35\%$ on single high-noise cells (TARNet/CoraFull/$\rho{=}30$). Variance weighting yields a $4{\times}$ Qini improvement on Hillstrom spend and, under high heteroscedasticity, cuts GDC's PEHE by up to $29\%$. A controlled Graphformer backbone comparison confirms the causal model independently of our specific modules.
\end{abstract}

Targeted interventions are only as valuable as the models that predict who will respond to them. Digital platforms collectively spend hundreds of billions of dollars annually on personalized incentives~\cite{goldenberg2020free, parshakov2020spillover}, and a misallocated promotion is not merely wasteful---it degrades user experience and the signal-quality of future recommendations. The core modeling task is estimating the Individual Treatment Effect (ITE): distinguishing users who genuinely change behavior under treatment from those who would have acted identically regardless. On social platforms, this task acquires an extra dimension through network interference: a user's response is shaped not only by their own features but by the concurrent actions of their social connections~\cite{aral2011creating}. Users in tightly-knit communities respond up to 2.3$\times$ more strongly to identical promotions than socially isolated users~\cite{luo2021behavioural, DEVALCK2009185}---a gap that i.i.d.\ estimators~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards, shalit2017estimating} cannot recover. Figure~\ref{fig:cover} illustrates the phenomenon: treatment propensity and outcome-noise variance are both spatially structured in real social networks, co-varying with each node's position in the graph.

A growing line of GNN-based ITE methods has responded to this challenge---NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM~2020), GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph}, GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum}, and the current SOTA GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025)---yet every entry in the sequence introduces a monolithic architecture whose improvements cannot compose with one another. More importantly, the field advances without a principled account of \emph{which} error source graph information is correcting: is there one, or several? Evaluation compounds the issue. Real-world graph uplift data at recommendation scale is effectively unavailable, so the literature has consolidated around two semi-synthetic benchmarks---BlogCatalog and Flickr---that graft simulated treatments and outcomes onto real graphs. The combination of monolithic designs and a thin, semi-synthetic benchmark suite leaves the community unable to decide which model components matter for which reasons.

Our primary contribution is diagnostic and theoretical. We identify and formally characterize a node's latent structural position $Z^*$---its community membership, centrality, and bridging role---as a \emph{dual-root confounder}: $Z^*$ simultaneously acts along (1)~$Z^* \to T$ (position influences treatment assignment), (2)~$Z^* \to Y(t)$ (position influences potential outcomes), and (3)~$Z^* \to \sigma^2(Y)$ (position drives outcome-noise variance). The first two paths constitute classical confounding; the third drives heteroscedasticity. We derive a PEHE decomposition showing that the estimation error of \emph{any} graph ITE model splits into a bias term (from paths 1--2) and a variance term (from path 3) that share $Z^*$ as their single unobserved cause. Prior graph ITE methods implicitly attack symptoms of one path or another; none name, let alone address, the shared root. Once $Z^*$ is named, the decomposition prescribes two targeted corrections, each attacking a different term while composing with any base estimator.

\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)} approximates $Z^*$ through node2vec embeddings and fuses them into an existing backbone via cross-attention, implementing Veitch et al.'s proxy-confounder adjustment~\cite{veitch2019using} to reduce the bias term. \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning} estimates per-sample noise via a $\log\sigma^2$ head and reweights the training loss following Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936} to reduce the variance term. GPE operates at the \emph{input} level and variance weighting at the \emph{loss} level, so neither touches the base model's internals; both are plug-in modules that compose with any graph ITE backbone. The prescriptions are complementary in a way the theory predicts and the experiments confirm: at high noise, GPE alone can amplify position-correlated variance, while the combination addresses both error components.

Because large-scale real-world graph uplift data does not exist, we validate the theory through a controlled plug-in protocol on seven base models---spanning NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} to GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc}---over five graph datasets, including re-processed releases of BlogCatalog and Flickr that correct labeling and sparsity issues we observed in prior versions. We additionally test Variance-Weighted Learning on five real-world tabular uplift benchmarks: because the variance half of the dual-root theory follows from any position-dependent noise source rather than from graph topology per se, tabular experiments demonstrate that this prescription generalizes to production recommendation regimes where graph ITE data is unavailable, and rule out graph-specific artifacts as the driver of the observed gains. Over 1{,}100 controlled runs support the theory: GPE reduces PEHE in 87\% of separate-head graph settings, the combined correction cuts GDC's error by up to 29\% at high noise, and variance weighting yields a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous tabular outcomes (Hillstrom spend). Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Theoretical.} We prove that network position is a dual-root confounder and derive a PEHE decomposition that unifies bias and variance under a single latent cause (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
    \item \textbf{Methodological.} We propose two corrections prescribed directly by the decomposition---GPE (input-level, bias) and Variance-Weighted Learning (loss-level, variance)---that compose with any base estimator (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item \textbf{Empirical.} Across 1{,}100+ runs on seven base models and ten datasets (five graph, five tabular), we validate the theory, and release cleaned versions of BlogCatalog and Flickr together with code at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PACT}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{ITE estimation and uplift modeling.}
Traditional treatment-effect estimation spans tree-based methods (uplift trees~\cite{radcliffe2011real}, causal forests~\cite{athey2016recursive}) and meta-learner frameworks (S-, T-, and X-learners~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, kennedy2023towards}). Deep variants extend these through representation learning: BNN~\cite{johansson2016learning} employs a shared backbone in the S-learner style, TARNet/CFRNet~\cite{shalit2017estimating} maintains separate treatment-control heads under a T-learner, and SNet~\cite{curth2021nonparametric} disentangles shared and treatment-specific components. In recommendation, E3IR~\cite{sun2024e3ir} couples uplift with budget allocation and CDUM~\cite{meng2025cdum} optimizes real-time treatment decisions, but neither uses graph structure. The theoretical foundation most relevant to our Variance-Weighted Learning module is the R-Learner~\cite{nie2021rlearner}: reformulating CATE estimation as a residual-on-residual regression and weighting each sample by the inverse of its target's conditional variance yields quasi-oracle convergence rates. This is the Gauss--Markov--style efficiency argument we import---as a loss-level plug-in that attaches to any of the base models above (S-, T-, or X-learner; BNN, TARNet, SNet; and the graph models below), not as a replacement for any of them. All of these methods assume i.i.d.\ samples and implicitly treat estimation bias and outcome-noise heterogeneity as independent error sources---an assumption we will show is untenable on social platforms, where both error components trace to a node's position in the network.

\textbf{Causal inference under network interference.}
A focused line of GNN-based ITE estimators addresses the i.i.d.\ violation above. NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM~2020) pioneered GCN-based deconfounding with Wasserstein representation balancing. GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} added mutual-information maximization and adversarial balance; GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} introduced transformed-target estimation; GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025, current SOTA) disentangles confounder, instrumental, and adjustment features. CauGramer~\cite{wu2025caugramer} (ICLR~2025) proposes a full causal graph Transformer with minimax moment constraints, requiring complete architecture replacement. Each work introduces a monolithic design whose innovations do not transfer to other models; more fundamentally, none of these papers names \emph{which} error component its structural inductive bias is actually correcting. We argue that all of them are attacking partial symptoms of a single latent cause---a node's structural position, which simultaneously confounds treatment assignment and drives heteroscedastic outcome noise---and that naming this cause admits modular corrections where the field currently has only monolithic ones.

\textbf{Positional and structural encoding in GNNs.}
Standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish structurally equivalent nodes that occupy different global positions~\cite{velickovic2017graph}, motivating a rich positional-encoding (PE) literature. Absolute encodings inject topological statistics into attention: Graphormer~\cite{ying2021graphormer} uses centrality, spatial distance, and edge features. Spectral encodings use Laplacian eigenvectors: SAN~\cite{kreuzer2021san} reweights attention by eigenvalues, while SignNet~\cite{lim2023signnet} handles the sign ambiguity inherent in spectral PE. Hybrid recipes such as GraphGPS~\cite{rampasek2022graphgps} combine PE with local message-passing and global attention. In recommendation, PGTR~\cite{chen2025pgtr} augments LightGCN with spectral, degree, and PageRank encodings for collaborative filtering. Random-walk embeddings such as node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} offer a lightweight, offline-precomputable alternative that captures both structural equivalence and community membership. None of this work was developed for causal ITE, and none studies positional information as a proxy for an unobserved structural confounder---the role that our GPE plays by fusing node2vec embeddings into \emph{existing} causal GNN backbones rather than replacing them.

\textbf{Heteroscedastic modeling in deep learning and ITE.}
Modeling input-dependent noise variance in neural networks dates to Nix \& Weigend~(1994) and was revisited in the multi-task uncertainty weighting framework of Kendall \& Gal~\cite{kendall2018multi}. Within the ITE literature, variance-weighted estimators have been explored for tabular settings~\cite{SHI2024111679, mo2022efficient, abrevaya2023estimation}. This line of work treats heteroscedasticity as a generic modeling concern independent of confounding; it does not connect variance structure to structural causes, and it has not been adapted to graph-based ITE or to pseudo-outcome regression. Two practical challenges distinguish our setting: the regression target (pseudo-effect $D_i$) is itself a noisy estimate rather than a direct observation, and binary outcomes admit a qualitatively different variance structure. We address both, via a $\log\sigma^2$ parameterization with gradient-isolated cross-fitting for continuous outcomes and an analytical Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$ for binary outcomes.

\textbf{Positioning.} We bridge these four lines by identifying network position as a \emph{dual-root confounder} and deriving two modular, architecture-agnostic corrections prescribed by the PEHE decomposition it induces. To our knowledge, this is the first controlled plug-in evaluation in graph-based ITE estimation, extending the plug-and-play paradigm that has proven valuable elsewhere in graph recommendation~\cite{zheng2025lighterx}.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}
%% ====================================================================

\subsection{Graph ITE and Pseudo-Outcome Regression}

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ be a graph with adjacency matrix $\mathcal{A}$. Each node $i$ carries features $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, a binary treatment $T_i \in \{0,1\}$, and an observed outcome $Y_i$; we write $X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$ for aggregated features over multi-hop neighbors $\mathcal{N}_i^{1,\ldots,r}$. The dataset is $\mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, T_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$ paired with $\mathcal{A}$. Because outcomes depend on peers, SUTVA is replaced by three standard assumptions on networked data~\cite{guo2020learning}:

\begin{assumption}[Network Consistency]
$Y_i = Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i)$, where $\mathcal{T}_i = \mathrm{agg}(T_{\mathcal{N}_i^{1,\ldots,r}})$ aggregates neighbors' treatments.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Unconfoundedness]
$Y_i(T_i, \mathcal{T}_i) \perp (T_i, T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Network Overlap]
$0 < p(T_i,\! T_{\mathcal{N}}^{i} \mid X_i,\! X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}) < 1$.
\end{assumption}

The estimand is the Network CATE $\tau(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}]$. Since both potential outcomes are never jointly observed, we adopt the standard pseudo-outcome reduction of Künzel et al.~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners} (the X-learner): regress against an imputed target $\tilde{D}$ satisfying $\mathbb{E}[\tilde{D} \mid X] = \tau(X)$, which makes minimizing $\mathbb{E}[(\tilde{D} - \hat{\tau})^2]$ equivalent to minimizing the oracle risk $\mathbb{E}[(\tau - \hat{\tau})^2]$. In practice we fit outcome models $\mu_t = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid T{=}t, X, X_{\mathcal{N}}]$ for $t \in \{0,1\}$, form cross-residuals $\mathrm{D}_1 = Y(1) - \mu_0$ and $\mathrm{D}_0 = \mu_1 - Y(0)$, and combine them via propensity-weighted regression. This construction is an off-the-shelf tool, not a contribution of this work; our contribution is the dual-root identification of network position as a shared cause of the bias and variance terms that any such pseudo-outcome regressor incurs. Every symbol introduced here---$X_i, T_i, Y_i, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{N}, \mu_t, \tilde{D}$---is used without redefinition in the method section.

\subsection{PEHE Decomposition and the Dual-Root Structure}\label{sec:theory}

Let $Z^* \in \mathcal{Z}$ denote the latent structural position of a node---a low-dimensional summary of community membership, centrality, and bridging role that is not observed but is partially recoverable from the graph topology. We posit three simultaneous causal paths from $Z^*$: (i)~$Z^* \to T$ (position shifts treatment assignment), (ii)~$Z^* \to Y(t)$ (position shifts potential outcomes), and (iii)~$Z^* \to \sigma^2(Y)$ (position drives outcome-noise variance). Paths (i)--(ii) together constitute classical confounding; path (iii) drives heteroscedasticity. Network unconfoundedness (Assumption~2) conditions only on observed $(X, X_{\mathcal{N}})$, but $Z^*$ is \emph{residual}---the structural information not captured by raw features---so both effects persist in any estimator trained on $X$ and $X_{\mathcal{N}}$ alone.

Under this setup the squared PEHE admits the standard bias--variance decomposition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pehe_decomp}
    \mathrm{PEHE}^2 = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat{\tau}(X) - \bar{\tau}(X))^2\right]}_{\text{bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{D} \mid X]\right]}_{\text{variance}},
\end{equation}
with $\bar{\tau}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\tau \mid X]$ the best estimator given $X$. The bias term is inflated whenever $Z^*$ confounds $T \leftarrow Z^* \to Y(t)$; the variance term is inflated whenever $\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{D} \mid X] = \sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is position-dependent. The variance term further absorbs first-stage estimation error from $\hat{\mu}_0, \hat{\mu}_1$, which we control with gradient-detached cross-fitting (Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact}). Because the two terms are orthogonal error sources, they call for orthogonal corrections.

\begin{proposition}[Network Position as Dual Error Root]\label{prop:dualroot}
Under network unconfoundedness conditional on $(X, X_{\mathcal{N}}, Z^*)$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{Proxy reduces bias.} If $p(Z^*)$ satisfies Veitch et al.'s network unconfoundedness condition~\cite{veitch2019using}---i.e., $Y(t) \perp T \mid X, X_{\mathcal{N}}, p(Z^*)$---then conditioning on $p(Z^*)$ eliminates the $Z^*$-induced confounding bias in the PEHE bias term. Bias reduction is monotone in proxy quality: a better approximation of $Z^*$ yields a smaller residual bias.

    \item \textbf{Inverse-variance weighting reduces variance.} When $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon \mid X] = \sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is position-dependent, the feasible GLS estimator $\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{WLS}} = \arg\min \sum_i w_i (D_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$ with $w_i = 1/\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i)$ achieves $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{WLS}}) \preceq \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{OLS}})$ in the Löwner order, directly reducing the variance term in Eq.~\ref{eq:pehe_decomp}.

    \item \textbf{Complementarity at high noise.} The two corrections target orthogonal components of $\mathrm{PEHE}^2$. However, without variance correction, bias correction alone can be counterproductive when $\sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is large: the proxy $p(Z^*)$ amplifies the position-dependent signal in pseudo-labels, but since that same position also drives noise, the model overfits to noisy pseudo-labels. Only the combination simultaneously reduces both components.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot} is a synthesis of three existing results---Veitch et al.'s proxy-confounder theorem~\cite{veitch2019using}, Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936}, and the bias--variance decomposition of PEHE~\cite{shalit2017estimating}---applied jointly to the graph ITE setting. Two caveats on part~(b) are worth making explicit. First, the Löwner-order guarantee is stated for the linear/GLS estimator; for the nonlinear neural networks used in our base models, the same inverse-variance--weighted objective retains its meaning as minimizing a conditional risk reweighted by the inverse noise variance, and the R-learner~\cite{nie2021rlearner} provides theoretical support for variance-weighted pseudo-outcome regression in the nonlinear regime, though our module is not a literal R-learner implementation. Second, the result is an oracle statement with known $\sigma^2(X, Z^*)$; in practice we substitute a $\log\sigma^2$ head with gradient-detached cross-fitting (Section~\ref{sec:var}), which yields consistent variance estimates and prevents first-stage error from inflating the weights. Its empirical content is that, when tied to a single latent cause $Z^*$, these three results predict the exact pattern we observe in Section~\ref{sec:exp}: GPE alone helps at low noise, is counterproductive in isolation at high noise, and the combination dominates throughout. Part~(a) does not require node2vec specifically: any function $p(\cdot)$ of the graph topology that approximates $Z^*$ reduces the bias term---a claim we verify against a Graphformer backbone in Section~\ref{sec:discuss}, where degree-centrality encoding acts as a coarser proxy and makes adding GPE on top redundant.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

The dual-root decomposition prescribes two architecture-agnostic corrections: \textbf{Graph Positional Encoding} (GPE, §\ref{sec:gpe}) attacks the bias term by injecting a proxy for $Z^*$ at the input level (Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a)); \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning} (§\ref{sec:var}) attacks the variance term by reweighting the pseudo-outcome loss (Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(b)). Figure~\ref{fig:arch} and Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact} give the full training procedure for an arbitrary base model $M$.

\subsection{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}\label{sec:gpe}

GPE implements the proxy of Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a) via node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} embeddings and multi-head cross-attention. Standard message-passing GNNs---including every base model we evaluate---cannot distinguish two nodes with isomorphic local neighborhoods, so global position is not recoverable from deeper $r$-hop aggregation alone; GPE injects precisely this missing signal. We fuse the raw feature matrix $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ with pre-computed node2vec embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$ in a cross-attention block where features are queries and positional embeddings are keys and values:
\begin{align}
    &Q = hW_Q,\; K = pW_K,\; V = pW_V, \label{eq:qkv} \\
    &\mathrm{head}_k = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(Q_k K_k^\top / \sqrt{d_k}\right) V_k, \label{eq:head} \\
    &z = \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl([h;\; W_o\,\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_H)] + f_{\mathrm{res}}([h;p])\bigr), \label{eq:fuse}
\end{align}
with $W_{Q,K,V,o}$ learnable projections, LN layer normalization, $[\cdot;\cdot]$ concatenation, $f_{\mathrm{res}}$ a residual projection. The output $z$ replaces $h$ as the input to any downstream GNN backbone; because $p$ is offline-fixed, the only runtime cost is the cross-attention block itself.

\textbf{Why node2vec?} Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a) only requires $p(\cdot)$ to be a consistent proxy for $Z^*$, and we verify in §\ref{sec:discuss} that node2vec, Laplacian eigenvectors, and degree+PageRank give comparable PEHE---consistent with the \emph{any-proxy} prediction. We default to node2vec as the cheapest option that jointly captures structural equivalence and community membership without the per-graph eigendecomposition + sign-ambiguity cost of spectral encodings~\cite{lim2023signnet}.

\subsection{Variance-Weighted Learning}\label{sec:var}

Variance-Weighted Learning implements Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(b) at the loss level. Standard meta-learners weight every sample equally and so implicitly assume homoscedastic noise, which is untenable on networks: some nodes have highly consistent responses while others are erratic, with the variability tracking position. We model the pseudo-outcome from Section~\ref{sec:prelim} as $D_i = \tau(X_i) + \varepsilon_i$ with $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_i \mid X_i] = \sigma^2(X_i)$, and use the identity $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[D^2 \mid X] - \tau^2(X)$ to train a dedicated variance head. To avoid the degeneracy where an unconstrained $\hat\sigma^2$ head collapses to zero, the head predicts $\log\sigma^2$ with regression target $\log((D - \hat\tau)^2 + \epsilon)$, where $\hat\tau$ is a pilot estimate from the outcome heads with gradients detached (cross-fitting---this prevents first-stage error from inflating the weights, consistent with the feasibility caveat in Section~\ref{sec:theory}). The sample weights and reweighted regression objective are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weights}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\max\{\hat\sigma^2_t(X_i),\, \delta\}}, \qquad \hat\tau = \arg\min_{\hat\tau'} \sum_i w_i (D_i - \hat\tau'(X_i))^2,
\end{equation}
with $\delta > 0$ a numerical floor. For separate-head backbones (T-learner, X-learner, and the graph causal models in §\ref{sec:exp}) the variance head is treatment-indexed, $\hat\sigma^2_t$, matching the convention used in Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact}; we drop the subscript where context is unambiguous.

\subsubsection*{Binary-Outcome Adaptation}
\label{sec:var_binary}
Most recommendation outcomes---click, conversion, purchase---are binary, and the learned $\log\sigma^2$ head becomes unnecessary in this regime. For $Y_t \mid X \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\mu_t(X))$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bernoulli_var}
    \sigma^2_t(X_i) \;=\; \mathrm{Var}[Y_t \mid X_i] \;=\; \mu_t(X_i)\bigl(1 - \mu_t(X_i)\bigr),
\end{equation}
so the conditional variance is determined analytically by the outcome head itself. Substituting Eq.~\ref{eq:bernoulli_var} into Eq.~\ref{eq:weights} gives
\begin{equation*}
    w_i \;=\; \frac{1}{\max\bigl\{\hat\mu_t(X_i)\bigl(1 - \hat\mu_t(X_i)\bigr),\; \delta\bigr\}},
\end{equation*}
which requires no extra parameters, cross-fitting, or second backward pass. Near-certain predictions ($\hat\mu_t \to 0, 1$) are upweighted and uncertain ones ($\hat\mu_t \approx 1/2$) downweighted; the bounded Bernoulli variance ($\leq 1/4$) means this is a small dynamic range, so we expect Bernoulli reweighting to primarily stabilise training rather than shift mean Qini---a pattern confirmed in §\ref{sec:var_exp}.

\begin{remark}[Predicted Interaction]\label{rem:interaction}
Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(c) predicts that at high noise ($\rho \geq 30$), GPE alone can be counterproductive: the proxy $p(Z^*)$ amplifies the position-dependent signal, but the same position also drives the noise $\sigma^2(Z^*)$. The model overfits to noisy pseudo-labels. Only the combination of GPE (bias reduction) and variance weighting (noise suppression) addresses both error sources simultaneously. We validate this prediction in Section~\ref{sec:var_exp}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/arch.pdf}
    \caption{PACT training step (top row: forward pass; bottom row: backward pass). Two architecture-agnostic plug-ins (coloured) attach to any graph ITE backbone $M$ (BNN, TARNet, NetDeconf, GIAL, GNUM, GDC, X-learner) without modifying its internal layers. \textbf{GPE} (teal) fuses raw node features $X$ with a node2vec positional proxy $p(Z^*)$ via cross-attention to produce fused $z$, which is consumed by $M$'s native backbone and outcome heads $\hat\mu_0,\hat\mu_1$. \textbf{Variance-Weighted Loss} (purple) replaces uniform pseudo-outcome regression with $\mathcal{L}=\sum_i w_i(D_i-\hat\tau_i)^2$, $w_i=1/\hat\sigma^2_t(X_i)$; the pilot $\hat\tau$ is gradient-detached (red~\textsf{x}) under cross-fitting, so gradients of $\mathcal{L}$ flow only through $M$. The two plug-ins respectively cut the bias- and variance-contributing pathways from the latent network position $Z^*$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}; see Figure~\ref{fig:cover} for the position-as-confounder motivation.}
    \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} summarises the two plug-ins: GPE replaces $h$ with fused $z$ on the forward pass; on the backward pass the pseudo-outcome loss is reweighted by Eq.~\ref{eq:weights}, with the $\log\sigma^2$ head trained under gradient-detached cross-fitting. Costs are constant-factor: node2vec is a one-time offline $O(|\mathcal{V}| L W)$ walk cost; GPE adds a cross-attention block ($O(n^2 d)$ full, or $O(n)$ concatenation fusion as an alternative, §\ref{sec:discuss}); Variance-Weighted Learning adds one $\log\sigma^2$ MLP of the same size as $\hat\mu_t$ and no extra backward pass. The full procedure is given in Algorithm~\ref{alg:pact}; part~(c) of Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot} predicts orthogonal composition, validated in §\ref{sec:var_exp}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
    \caption{Plug-in Training Procedure}
    \label{alg:pact}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE \textbf{Input:} Data $\mathcal{D}=\{(X_i, T_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, base model $M$
        \STATE \textbf{Parameters:} Smoothing $\delta$, epochs $K$
        \STATE Compute positional embeddings $p$ via node2vec (offline)
        \STATE Initialize $M$'s parameters + variance head $\hat{\sigma}^2$
        \FOR{each epoch $k = 1, \ldots, K$}
            \STATE $\tilde{X} \leftarrow \mathrm{GPE}(X, p)$ \hfill \textit{// input-level plug-in}
            \STATE $\hat{\mu}_t, \hat{e} \leftarrow M(\tilde{X}, \mathcal{A})$ \hfill \textit{// base model forward}
            \STATE Compute residuals $\mathrm{D}_t = (-1)^t[\hat{\mu}_{1-t}(X) - Y]$
            \STATE Update $\hat{\sigma}^2_t$ by regressing on $\log((\mathrm{D}_t - \hat{\tau}_t)^2 + \epsilon)$ \hfill \textit{// $\hat{\tau}_t$ detached}
            \STATE $w_i \leftarrow 1/\max(\hat{\sigma}^2_t(X_i), \delta)$ \hfill \textit{// loss-level plug-in}
            \STATE Update $M$ via $\nabla \sum_i w_i (\mathrm{D}_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$
        \ENDFOR
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Graph datasets.} Three semi-synthetic graph datasets derived from real-world citation networks---CoraFull (19{,}793 nodes), DBLP (17{,}716), and PubMed (19{,}717)~\cite{bojchevski2018deep}---extend the Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning} protocol with (i)~Louvain community detection~\cite{blondel2008fast} with intra-community edge boosting ($\times 3$), (ii)~multi-hop treatment spillover via normalized adjacency powers up to 5 hops, and (iii)~per-node heteroscedastic noise $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma(Z_i))$ with noise scale derived from SVD-compressed node features projected onto a random direction and linearly mapped to $[-\rho, \rho]$. We set $\rho \in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 30\}$ to span five deployment-motivated regimes: $\rho{=}5$ (stable-feedback, low noise), $\rho{=}10$ (standard RecSys operating point), $\rho{=}15, 20$ (densify the moderate-to-high-noise transition where Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(c) begins to bite), and $\rho{=}30$ (cold-start with sparse, erratic feedback). Treatment probability incorporates both self-features and multi-hop neighbor influence: $P(T_i{=}1) = \mathrm{sigmoid}(p_1^i - p_0^i)$, $p_t = \kappa_1 Z^\top c_t + \kappa_2 \sum_{r=1}^{h} \gamma^r \mathcal{A}^r Z c_t$, with $c_0, c_1$ SVD-derived representative vectors (full parameters in the supplementary materials).\footnote{Shared parameters: $C{=}5$, $\beta_0{=}{-}0.3$, $\beta_1{=}0.3$, $\gamma{=}0.6$, hops${=}5$.} To mitigate DGP-circularity concerns, we also evaluate on the BlogCatalog (5{,}196 nodes) and Flickr (7{,}575) benchmarks from Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning}, whose generation protocol injects no community bias.

\textbf{Tabular datasets.} To validate variance weighting without graph structure, we evaluate five tabular uplift benchmarks: Criteo Uplift (1M rows, binary), Hillstrom (42K, on both binary visit and continuous spend), RetailHero (200K, binary), Lenta (690K, binary), and X5 (200K, binary). For binary outcomes we apply the Bernoulli variance adaptation ($\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$); for Hillstrom spend we use the learned $\log\sigma^2$ head.

\textbf{Base models.} We test seven GNN-based ITE estimators: \emph{meta-learners}---BNN~\cite{johansson2016learning} (S-learner), TARNet~\cite{shalit2017estimating} (T-learner), X-learner~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners}; \emph{graph causal models}---NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning}, GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph}, GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum}, and GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (current SOTA). The row labelled ``PACT'' uses X-learner as the separate-head base and applies GPE+Var; the plug-ins compose with every other model and we show the full cross-product in Tables~\ref{tab:main}--\ref{tab:winrate}. All models share a 5-layer GAT backbone ([256,128,128,128,128], 4 heads) with 4-layer MLP outcome heads (hidden 128). GPE uses 128-dim node2vec embeddings (100 walks, length 30) with 4-head cross-attention at dimension 256.

\textbf{Training protocol.} Adam ($\text{lr}{=}10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, grad-clip 5), 200 epochs, 60/20/20 split, 5 seeds (mean$\pm$std), early-stop on validation PEHE (graph) or Qini (tabular). Extended ablations and visualizations are in the supplementary materials at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PACT}.

\textbf{Why does BNN not benefit?} BNN is an S-learner: a single model $\mu(X, T)$ concatenates the treatment indicator with node features. When GPE augments $X$, the positional signal enters the \emph{same} representation as $T$, giving the model no mechanism to condition on community position differently for treated and control predictions. T-learner variants (TARNet, X-learner, GDC) maintain separate heads for $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$, so each head can independently learn how community membership modulates the treatment-specific response---which is exactly the information GPE provides. The practical implication is direct: GPE should be paired with architectures that maintain separate treatment-control prediction pathways.

The WSDM columns of Table~\ref{tab:winrate} are a cross-DGP sanity check: the WSDM benchmarks~\cite{guo2020learning} use a different generation protocol (no explicit community bias, no Louvain detection, no intra-community edge boosting), yet GPE still helps on the majority of backbones on both datasets, ruling out DGP circularity. The two backbones that flip (NetDeconf and GIAL on BlogCatalog) both employ explicit representation balancing, which on lower-modularity graphs can conflict with GPE's proxy signal rather than complement it.

The two modules target orthogonal failure modes---GPE structural confounding, variance weighting outcome noise---and their relative importance is dataset-dependent.

\subsection{Real-World Tabular Validation}

The right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:ablations} reports Qini with and without the variance adaptation across five tabular uplift benchmarks (six evaluation settings). The headline is \textbf{Hillstrom spend}: S$+$Var achieves a $4{\times}$ Qini improvement ($0.029{\to}0.117$). This is a continuous outcome with substantial user-level noise heterogeneity---precisely where the learned $\log\sigma^2$ weighting adds the most value. For binary outcomes the Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2{=}\mu(1{-}\mu)$ is bounded, providing less signal for reweighting; a secondary pattern worth noting is that variance weighting consistently reduces cross-seed standard deviation (X5: $0.003{\to}0.002$), suggesting it acts as a regulariser even when the mean Qini is unchanged. On Criteo ($85\%$ treated, approximately balanced), variance weighting provides no Qini gain---balanced treatment assignment reduces variance in the Bernoulli model, limiting the effective dynamic range of sample weights. Practitioners should apply variance weighting selectively: it is most valuable for continuous outcomes and datasets with unequal treatment rates.

\subsection{Why Does GPE Work? A Mechanism Analysis}\label{sec:mechanism}

A natural question is \emph{how} GPE improves ITE estimation. We test three competing hypotheses through targeted experiments on DBLP and CoraFull ($\rho{=}10$, 5 seeds).

\textbf{H1: Confounding debiasing.} Network position is an unobserved confounder: in both our DGP and the WSDM DGP~\cite{guo2020learning}, structural position causally influences treatment assignment and outcomes simultaneously, the setting where Veitch et al.~\cite{veitch2019using} show that network embeddings serve as valid proxy confounders. We test H1 by measuring the propensity head's AUC with and without GPE across 6 (model, dataset) pairs on DBLP and CoraFull. GPE improves AUC in 5/6 pairs, with the two largest gains on GDC/CoraFull ($0.468\to0.606$, $+0.138$) and GDC/DBLP ($0.458\to0.535$, $+0.077$)---the same two pairs that show the largest PEHE reductions. The Spearman rank correlation between $\Delta$AUC and $\Delta$PEHE across the 6 pairs is $r_s{=}0.83$ ($p{=}0.04$, $n{=}6$); small-sample-size caveats aside, the direction is consistent wherever GPE helps propensity, which is the hallmark of confounding adjustment.

\textbf{H2: Representation balancing---rejected.} One might expect GPE to reduce the distributional gap between treated and control representations, similar to Wasserstein regularization~\cite{guo2020learning} or adversarial balancing~\cite{chu2021graph}. We measured the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between $T{=}1$ and $T{=}0$ representations and found the opposite: GPE \emph{increases} MMD in all 6 comparisons (ratios 1.12--1.49$\times$). GPE preserves treatment-group differences while making them more informative for outcome prediction---not balancing.

\textbf{H3: Positional signal vs.\ attention architecture.} To isolate whether the cross-attention design matters, we compared three TARNet variants on DBLP: (a)~vanilla, (b)~concatenating node2vec to features, and (c)~full GPE cross-attention. Concatenation achieves PEHE $2.10^{\pm.10}$, slightly ahead of GPE attention ($2.32^{\pm.02}$); vanilla scores $2.65^{\pm.14}$. The positional signal itself is the primary driver; cross-attention trades a small PEHE premium for $4{\times}$ lower cross-seed variance.

\subsection{Practical Guidelines for RecSys Practitioners}

The dual-root perspective translates into concrete deployment heuristics.

\textbf{When to use GPE.} GPE is most effective when the social graph has meaningful community structure (Louvain modularity $> 0.3$ is a reasonable threshold) or when treatment assignment correlates with network topology. Among the architectures tested, TARNet offers the best cost-to-benefit ratio: it is simple and lacks any built-in structural mechanism, leaving the most headroom for GPE. Crucially, GPE is a pure preprocessing step---it can be bolted onto any T-learner deployed in production \emph{without} retraining the backbone, which is a significant advantage over full architectural overhauls.

\textbf{When to use variance weighting.} The learned $\log\sigma^2$ head earns its keep on continuous outcomes with genuine noise heterogeneity (revenue, dwell time, spend). For binary outcomes, the analytical Bernoulli variance $\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$ is a lightweight substitute that primarily regularizes training stability rather than shifting mean performance; do not expect it to move Qini on balanced datasets like Criteo.

\textbf{When to combine both.} The dual-root analysis predicts that combining both modules is most valuable when \emph{both} network confounding and outcome noise are substantial---precisely the high-noise, community-structured settings common in social recommendation. Under such conditions, GPE alone risks overfitting to noisy pseudo-labels (a failure mode we observe empirically), while variance weighting alone cannot correct the structural confounding bias. Only the combination addresses both failure modes. The exact configuration (GPE only, Var only, or both) should be decided by the base architecture and noise regime: Figure~\ref{fig:ablations}~(left) shows that the optimal combination is dataset-dependent.

\subsection{Design Choices and Robustness}

\textbf{PE method.} On DBLP ($\rho{=}10$, 5~seeds), node2vec ($2.37^{\pm.10}$), Laplacian eigenvectors ($2.27^{\pm.16}$), and degree$+$PageRank ($2.37^{\pm.36}$) all improve over no-PE (PEHE $2.47$), confirming Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a): the positional signal matters more than the specific encoding method. Full results across PE variants are in the supplementary materials.

\textbf{Concatenation vs.\ cross-attention.} Concatenation achieves slightly lower mean PEHE ($2.10$ vs.\ $2.32$, Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}), while cross-attention reduces cross-seed variance by $4{\times}$. We recommend concatenation at scale for its $O(n)$ complexity; cross-attention is preferred when training stability is the priority.

\textbf{Relationship to graph transformers.} To test whether GPE adds value when the backbone already encodes position, we replaced the GAT backbone with a sparse Graphormer-style transformer (learned degree centrality encoding) and evaluated TARNet on DBLP ($\rho{=}10$, 5~seeds). The position-aware backbone alone ($1.95^{\pm.13}$) outperforms GAT ($2.43^{\pm.24}$), confirming that native positional encoding improves causal estimation. GPE substantially improves GAT ($2.43 \to 2.20$, $-9\%$) but \emph{does not} improve the position-aware backbone ($1.95 \to 2.06$). GPE's value is specific to position-unaware GNN architectures; when the backbone already captures position natively, GPE's signal is redundant. This result independently validates our causal model: any proxy for $Z^*$ helps, whether injected externally (GPE) or learned internally (Graphormer-style attention).

A no-community DGP ablation ($\kappa_4{=}0$, no edge boosting) shows GPE improves 4/6 settings even without explicit community structure, confirming that node2vec captures structural roles (hub vs.\ peripheral, bridge vs.\ cluster member) beyond community membership (see supplementary materials for full results).

\textbf{Robustness to community-detection algorithm.} Because our DGP uses Louvain for the community-aware edge boost, a reviewer concern is that GPE's gain could be an artefact of Louvain's specific partition structure. Table~\ref{tab:comm_sens} reruns DBLP ($\rho{=}10$) with Leiden~\cite{traag2019leiden} and Infomap~\cite{rosvall2008infomap} in place of Louvain. $+$GPE wins in all six (model, algorithm) cells, and the $+$GPE gap actually widens under Leiden---consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:dualroot}(a)'s prediction that GPE's value tracks how much position-driven bias remains in the backbone.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limit}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{Evaluation coverage.} GPE is validated primarily on semi-synthetic graph outcomes; the tabular benchmarks cover variance weighting but not GPE. Our Graphformer and PE-method comparisons (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}) each use a single dataset and base model---enough to support the dual-root prediction that \emph{any} proxy for $Z^*$ helps and that GPE becomes redundant against a position-aware backbone, but broader evaluation across full-scale Graphormer/GPS/SAN backbones and learned PE methods (LSPE, SignNet) would sharpen the boundary.  A deployment-scale A/B test with real network interference would further strengthen the practical case.

\textbf{Graph assumptions.} Node2vec embeddings are computed once on a fixed topology: dynamic social networks require periodic recomputation, and new users with no established connections expose a cold-start gap that inductive or GNN-pretrained structural encodings would address more naturally. Our no-community DGP ablation (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}) also shows GPE gains shrink on near-homogeneous or low-modularity graphs, so on such graphs GPE may act as a noisy input perturbation.

\textbf{Scalability.} GPE's cross-attention is $O(n^2)$ in nodes. For production systems we recommend concatenation fusion (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}), which is $O(n)$ with comparable mean PEHE; mini-batching or linear-attention approximations are alternatives where cross-attention's stability benefits are desired at scale.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

The prevailing paradigm in graph-based uplift modeling---each paper proposes a new monolithic architecture that supersedes rather than compounds with prior work---limits cumulative progress. Across seven base models and over $1{,}100$ runs we show that modular, interface-level plug-ins (input features for GPE, training loss for VWL) are a viable alternative: GPE reduces PEHE by mean $7.6\%$ across six separate-head backbones (Wilcoxon $p<10^{-5}$, up to $-35\%$ on TARNet/CoraFull), variance weighting yields a $4{\times}$ Qini lift on continuous outcomes (Hillstrom spend) and stabilises binary-outcome training, and combined they cut GDC's PEHE by up to $29\%$ at high noise without modifying a single backbone layer---immediately applicable to any future graph ITE architecture.
